# Act III: The Covenant

It is 2:15 p.m. on a Wednesday. The coffee shop on the corner of Bermondsey Street is warm and smells of espresso and damp coats, and the table Dev has claimed by the window is just far enough from the machine to make conversation possible. His americano is half-finished and has been for twenty minutes. On the screen: a blank document and, open in the other window, a GitHub notification thread he has been composing a response to in his head since Tuesday.

Dev Anand is 29. He maintains four open source projects and contributes to eleven others, which is the kind of thing that sounds impressive until someone asks him how many evenings it takes. He is originally from Leicester, currently in London, and has been writing code since he was fourteen in the methodical, slightly obsessive way of someone who finds that the logic of it settles something in him that most other things do not. He is not writing code right now. He is writing a Code of Conduct, which is a different kind of logic: the logic of a room that needs rules because the alternative has a known cost, and Dev has paid it.

The notification thread is from a first-time contributor who left a comment on a newcomer's PR eight months ago. The comment was not cruel. It was not targeted. It was the kind of terse, impatient dismissal that the person who wrote it almost certainly does not remember writing. The newcomer never came back. Dev noticed three weeks later when he went to reply to a follow-up question and found the account had gone quiet. He does not know for certain that the comment was the reason. He is certain enough.

He starts to type.

---

## The Pledge

We, the maintainers and contributors of Claude Sleuth, make a pledge to one another. It is not written in blood, nor sealed with wax, nor whispered into the ear of a dying informant on the steps of a Whitechapel pub at closing time. It is committed to `main`, which in many ways is more permanent.

This project is open to every person regardless of age, body, disability, ethnicity, gender identity, experience level, nationality, appearance, race, religion, or sexual orientation. The codebase does not care who you are. It cares whether your indentation is consistent.

Every participant deserves an environment free from harassment, contempt, and the particular breed of passive aggression that hides behind the phrase "per my last comment."

This pledge is non-negotiable. It is also, regrettably, not enforceable by Interpol.

---

## The Standards

### Behaviours That Build Trust

Dev thinks about the contributor who submitted a three-hundred-line refactor of the template builder with a description that said only "cleaned this up." He spent an hour in review trying to understand the reasoning. Then he wrote back, not with frustration, but with questions. The contributor explained the whole thing in twenty minutes. The PR merged. They have since submitted four more.

The following behaviours are what that exchange required of both parties, and what this project asks of everyone:

- Welcoming language, extended to strangers and regulars alike
- Constructive criticism delivered without malice and without the word "obviously"
- Empathy for people whose context, experience, and timezone are not yours
- Graceful acceptance of feedback, including the kind that requires a rewrite
- A bug report that includes reproduction steps, because "it doesn't work" is not a diagnosis

### Behaviours That Erode It

Dev thinks about the comment from eight months ago. Eleven words. Typed in thirty seconds, probably. It said nothing overtly wrong. It communicated, with complete efficiency, that the person who wrote it had no patience for the question being asked and considered the asking of it a minor imposition. The newcomer read it, responded once with an apology, and was never seen again.

The following behaviours are not tolerated here:

- Sexualised language or imagery in a space where nobody asked for it
- Trolling, insults, or the weaponised impatience of someone who has confused brevity with authority
- Publishing someone's private information without consent
- Sustained harassment or the exploitation of a power imbalance, however small and however deniable
- Any conduct a reasonable person would consider inappropriate in a professional setting, a public library, or a pub that has not yet had reason to ask anyone to leave

---

## Enforcement

Reports of unacceptable behaviour may be directed to the project maintainer at the contact address listed in the repository. Every report is reviewed. Confidentiality is maintained throughout.

The maintainer reserves the right to remove, edit, or reject comments, commits, code, issues, and other contributions that violate this Code. Temporary or permanent bans may be issued. These decisions are not made quickly, and they are not made on the basis of a single ambiguous sentence taken out of context. But they are made. And they are final.

---

## Scope

This Code of Conduct applies within all project spaces and extends to any public space where someone is acting as a representative of the project. The boundary is simple: if your conduct could reasonably be attributed to Claude Sleuth, this document applies.

Dev finishes the document at 3:04 p.m. The americano is cold. He reads it back once, changes two words, and commits it. Then he goes back to the notification thread and writes the response he has been composing since Tuesday. It is three sentences. It is direct and it is kind and it says exactly what he means.

He closes the laptop. Outside the window, Bermondsey Street carries on in the grey Wednesday light, indifferent and alive.
