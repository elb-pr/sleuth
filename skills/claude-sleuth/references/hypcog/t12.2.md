# t12.2 — Assessing Diagnosticity and Matrix Scoring

**Template:** `analysis/ach.md`

## Procedure

1. It is CRITICAL that you score each cell using the single, precisely framed question: **"Given that Hypothesis X is true, how likely is it that I would observe this evidence?"** Asking "Does this evidence support Hypothesis X?" is FORBIDDEN — it invites confirmation bias.

2. YOU MUST apply the five-tier scoring convention without deviation:
   - **CC** — Very Consistent: the evidence is strongly expected if this hypothesis is true.
   - **C** — Consistent: the evidence is plausible within this explanatory framework.
   - **N/A** — Not Applicable: no diagnostic relationship to this hypothesis.
   - **I** — Inconsistent: the evidence would be unexpected if this hypothesis is true.
   - **II** — Very Inconsistent: the evidence is strongly contradictory to this hypothesis.

3. ALWAYS score cells independently. The following are FORBIDDEN:
   - Scoring an entire row simultaneously (anchoring effect).
   - Letting the score of one hypothesis influence the score of another.
   - Scoring your favoured hypothesis first — YOU MUST begin with the hypothesis considered LEAST likely.

4. After initial scoring, YOU MUST prune the matrix:
   - Evidence rated C or CC against EVERY hypothesis lacks discriminatory power — flag for removal.
   - Evidence rated N/A against EVERY hypothesis is irrelevant — remove.
   - It is CRITICAL that pruned items are moved to an appendix with documented rationale — deletion is FORBIDDEN.

5. In multi-analyst teams: a second analyst MUST independently score a subset of cells. Significant disagreements MUST be discussed and resolved. You are REQUIRED to record the outcome.

6. ALWAYS ensure Admiralty grades are visible on each evidence row. High-grade inconsistencies carry greater analytical weight. Formal weighting is applied in t12.3 Sensitivity Analysis.
