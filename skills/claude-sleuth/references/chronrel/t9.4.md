# t9.4 — Chronological Sub-Types

**Template:** `analysis/chronological-matrix.md`

A single master chronology, while essential, is often insufficient for complex investigations. Different analytical questions require different temporal lenses. YOU MUST create specialised sub-timelines that extract specific event streams from the master chronology, enabling focused analysis of particular domains without losing the broader temporal context.

## Procedure

### 1. Financial Transaction Timelines
When the investigation involves suspected money laundering, fraud, asset concealment, or financial misconduct, YOU MUST produce a dedicated financial timeline isolating the movement of funds.
- You are REQUIRED to include: all financial events — bank transfers, cash deposits, invoice payments, property transactions, share transfers, loan agreements, and cryptocurrency movements — extracted from the master chronology and sequenced.
- ALWAYS map financial transactions alongside corporate events (incorporation, dissolution, director changes) and legal events (litigation filings, regulatory actions, insolvency proceedings) to expose coordination between financial and legal manoeuvres.
- It is CRITICAL that the financial timeline enables pattern analysis: regular transaction cadences that suddenly change, transfers immediately preceding or following corporate restructuring, or fund movements creating circular flows MUST be flagged.

### 2. Communication Timelines
When relationship dynamics, conspiracy, or coordination are central to the investigation, YOU MUST produce a communication timeline mapping the evolution of contact between entities.
- You are REQUIRED to include: all recorded communications — phone calls, text messages, emails, social media interactions, messaging app activity, and in-person meetings (where documented) — sequenced by timestamp.
- ALWAYS analyse relationship evolution: a sudden increase in contact frequency before a key event and sudden cessation after a key event MUST both be flagged as potential indicators of planning and distancing respectively.

### 3. Subject-Specific Timelines
For investigations with multiple subjects, YOU MUST produce individual timelines isolating each key entity's documented activity.
- You are REQUIRED to include: all events involving a specific person or organisation, extracted from the master chronology, producing a biographical-temporal profile showing movements, transactions, communications, and life events in sequence.
- In law enforcement contexts, YOU MUST format subject-specific timelines to satisfy prosecution file requirements (e.g., UK MG5 case summaries) and disclosure schedules.

### 4. Parallel and Comparative Timelines
YOU MUST produce parallel timelines placing two or more event streams side by side on the same temporal axis for every investigation where coordination between entities is a hypothesis.
- It is CRITICAL that parallel timelines are used for counter-narrative testing: if two witnesses provide conflicting accounts, YOU MUST place them on a shared timeline to expose internal contradictions or identify points of agreement.
- ALWAYS use the Time-Person Grid format (time intervals as columns, individuals as rows) for alibi analysis.

### 5. Sub-Timeline Governance
All sub-timelines are REQUIRED to be derivatives of the master chronology and MUST maintain full traceability:
- **Source Inheritance:** Every event on a sub-timeline MUST inherit its source attribution and confidence rating from the master chronology. An event appearing on a sub-timeline without a corresponding master chronology entry is FORBIDDEN.
- **Gap Propagation:** YOU MUST carry temporal gaps identified in t9.2 into all relevant sub-timelines. A financial timeline MUST show the same gap in transaction records that the master chronology identifies.
- **Cross-Reference:** Sub-timelines MUST be cross-referenceable — an event on a communication timeline MUST link to the same event's entry on the master chronology and, where relevant, to the corresponding entry on a financial or subject-specific timeline.
