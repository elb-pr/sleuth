Task 11.2, **Map Regression and Urban Morphology**, uses the **Place History Template** to analyse how specific locations have changed over time. By comparing sequences of historical maps, satellite imagery, and aerial photographs, the investigator can track physical development, land use changes, and structural alterations that provide contextual intelligence about the investigation's geographic focus areas.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Map Regression Methodology
Map regression is the systematic comparison of cartographic representations of the same location across different time periods to identify change.
*   **Source Acquisition:** The analyst assembles a chronological series of maps and imagery covering the location of interest. Sources include historical Ordnance Survey maps (UK), USGS topographic maps (US), declassified aerial photography, commercial satellite imagery archives (Google Earth historical imagery, Sentinel Hub), and institutional map collections.
*   **Temporal Layering:** Maps from different periods are aligned geographically (georeferenced) so that changes between periods can be identified through direct overlay comparison. Each map layer is dated and sourced.
*   **Change Identification:** The analyst systematically documents what has changed between each period: new construction, demolition, land use conversion (agricultural to industrial, residential to commercial), road network changes, boundary alterations, and landscape modification.

### 2. Urban Morphology Analysis
Urban morphology examines the physical form and structure of the built environment and how it has evolved.
*   **Building Footprint Analysis:** Changes in building footprints between map periods reveal construction timelines, expansion patterns, and demolition history. This is particularly valuable for investigations involving property development, planning fraud, or disputed land ownership.
*   **Land Parcel History:** Tracking how land parcels have been subdivided, merged, or rezoned provides intelligence about ownership changes and development intentions that may not be reflected in registry records alone.
*   **Infrastructure Context:** The development of roads, utilities, and transport links around a location of interest provides contextual intelligence about accessibility, economic development, and the practical logistics of activities at that location.

### 3. Investigative Applications
Map regression and urban morphology serve specific investigative functions:
*   **Property Investigation:** Tracing the physical development of a property to identify when structures were built, modified, or demolished — which can be compared against planning applications, building permits, and insurance records.
*   **Historical Context:** Understanding the historical character of a location to interpret archival evidence. A photograph of an entity at a specific address carries different analytical weight depending on whether that address was, at the time, a residential property, a commercial premises, or a vacant lot.
*   **Environmental and Planning Intelligence:** Identifying unauthorised construction, environmental damage, or planning violations through comparison of permitted development against observed physical changes.

### 4. Output: Place History Report
The analysis is compiled into a structured report mapping the temporal evolution of the location.
*   **Timeline of Physical Change:** A chronological record of documented changes, sourced from the map and imagery comparison.
*   **Current State Assessment:** A description of the location's present condition, verified against the most recent available imagery.
*   **Entity Register Integration:** The Place History Report is linked to the relevant Location entities in the Entity Register, enriching the spatial dimension of the investigation's data.
