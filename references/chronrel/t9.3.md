Task 9.3, **Conflict Resolution**, addresses the inevitable problem of multiple sources providing contradictory timestamps or accounts for the same event. In any investigation drawing on diverse source types — system logs, witness statements, social media posts, official records — temporal conflicts will arise. The analyst must resolve or formally document each conflict rather than silently choosing a preferred version.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Detecting Temporal Conflicts
Conflicts are identified during the assembly of the master chronology (Task 9.1) and fall into two categories:
*   **Timestamp Conflicts:** Two or more sources report different times for the same event. For example, a bank record timestamps a transaction at 14:23 UTC while a witness statement places the associated meeting at "around 2pm local time" (which could be 13:00 or 14:00 UTC depending on the timezone).
*   **Narrative Conflicts:** Two sources agree on the timing of an event but disagree on what happened. For example, two witnesses place a meeting at the same time and location but describe different attendees or outcomes.

### 2. Source Reliability Comparison
The primary resolution mechanism is the Admiralty grading system applied during Phase 2 (Task 3.1b).
*   **Technical vs Human Sources:** System-generated timestamps (bank transaction logs, email server headers, CCTV time-codes) are generally more reliable than human recollection. A server log timestamped to the millisecond outweighs a witness estimate of "around 2pm" in temporal precision.
*   **Primary vs Secondary Sources:** A source that directly recorded the event (e.g., the bank's own transaction log) is more reliable than a source reporting on the event second-hand (e.g., a news article describing the transaction).
*   **Admiralty Grade Comparison:** Where both sources have been graded, the higher-reliability source's timestamp takes precedence in the master chronology, but the conflicting timestamp is preserved with its source attribution.

### 3. Resolution Outcomes
Each conflict must terminate in one of three documented outcomes:
*   **Resolved — Preferred Timestamp:** The analyst determines that one source is more reliable and adopts its timestamp for the master chronology. The rejected timestamp is retained in the record with a note explaining why it was not preferred. The Temporal Certainty field is updated to "Resolved conflict — [source preferred]."
*   **Resolved — Reconciled:** The apparent conflict is explained by a non-contradictory interpretation. For example, a timezone error in one source, or two timestamps that actually refer to different phases of the same event (e.g., meeting start vs meeting end). The reconciliation is documented.
*   **Unresolved — Flagged for Further Investigation:** The analyst cannot determine which source is correct. Both timestamps are retained, the event is marked as "Conflicting" in the Temporal Certainty field, and a new Intelligence Requirement is generated to seek disambiguating evidence.

### 4. Analytical Significance of Conflicts
Temporal conflicts are not merely administrative problems — they can be analytically revealing.
*   **Deception Indicators:** If a subject's self-reported timeline systematically conflicts with independently verified timestamps, this pattern may indicate deliberate misrepresentation. The pattern itself becomes evidence for the ACH matrix in Phase 5.
*   **Source Reliability Reassessment:** Systematic inaccuracies from a specific source may require a downgrade of its Admiralty reliability rating (A–F). This reassessment cascades through the Source Register (Task 5.3), affecting the credibility of all information from that source.
*   **Alibi Destruction:** A conflict between a subject's claimed location (e.g., "I was at home") and a technical record (e.g., mobile phone tower data placing them elsewhere) is a high-priority conflict with direct investigative significance.

### 5. Documentation Standard
Every conflict and its resolution must be documented in sufficient detail to withstand external scrutiny.
*   **Conflict Register:** A sub-section of the master chronology or a separate annex listing all identified conflicts, the sources involved, the resolution outcome, and the reasoning. This register is available for supervisory review and legal disclosure.
*   **Transparency in Final Reporting:** Unresolved conflicts must be surfaced in the final briefing (Phase 6). The analyst cannot present a clean, unambiguous chronology if the underlying temporal evidence is disputed — the conflict itself must be communicated to the decision-maker.
