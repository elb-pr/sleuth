Task 9.4, **Chronological Types**, recognises that a single master chronology, while essential, is often insufficient for complex investigations. Different analytical questions require different temporal lenses. This task creates specialised sub-timelines that extract specific event streams from the master chronology, enabling focused analysis of particular domains without losing the context of the broader temporal picture.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Financial Transaction Timelines
When the investigation involves suspected money laundering, fraud, asset concealment, or financial misconduct, a dedicated financial timeline isolates the movement of funds.
*   **Content:** All financial events — bank transfers, cash deposits, invoice payments, property transactions, share transfers, loan agreements, and cryptocurrency movements — extracted from the master chronology and sequenced.
*   **Layering with Legal Events:** Financial transactions are mapped alongside corporate events (incorporation, dissolution, director changes) and legal events (litigation filings, regulatory actions, insolvency proceedings) to expose coordination between financial and legal manoeuvres.
*   **Anomaly Detection:** The financial timeline enables pattern analysis — regular transaction cadences that suddenly change, transfers that immediately precede or follow corporate restructuring, or fund movements that create circular flows.

### 2. Communication Timelines
When relationship dynamics, conspiracy, or coordination are central to the investigation, a communication timeline maps the evolution of contact between entities.
*   **Content:** All recorded communications — phone calls, text messages, emails, social media interactions, messaging app activity, and in-person meetings (where documented) — sequenced by timestamp.
*   **Relationship Evolution:** The communication timeline exposes how relationships develop over time. A sudden increase in contact frequency between two subjects before a key event may indicate planning or coordination. A sudden cessation of contact after a key event may indicate distancing.
*   **Network Dynamics:** When mapped alongside the network analysis in Step 10, communication timelines reveal whether network structures are stable or dynamic — whether alliances form, dissolve, or shift over the investigation period.

### 3. Subject-Specific Timelines
For investigations with multiple subjects, individual timelines isolate a single entity's documented activity.
*   **Content:** All events involving a specific person or organisation, extracted from the master chronology. This produces a biographical-temporal profile showing movements, transactions, communications, and life events in sequence.
*   **Prosecution and Disclosure Use:** In law enforcement contexts, subject-specific timelines are frequently required for prosecution files (e.g., UK MG5 case summaries) and disclosure schedules, as they present the evidence relevant to a specific defendant in an accessible chronological format.

### 4. Parallel and Comparative Timelines
The most analytically powerful timeline type places two or more event streams side by side on the same temporal axis.
*   **Coordination Detection:** A parallel timeline comparing Subject A's movements with Subject B's communications may reveal that Subject B made a phone call immediately before Subject A arrived at a specific location — suggesting coordination that neither individual timeline would expose in isolation.
*   **Time-Person Grid:** A matrix format with time intervals as columns and individuals as rows, showing simultaneous actions across the investigation's subjects. This format is particularly effective for alibi analysis and establishing who was where, when.
*   **Counter-Narrative Testing:** Parallel timelines are used to test competing accounts of events. If two witnesses provide conflicting narratives, placing their accounts on a shared timeline can expose internal contradictions or identify points of agreement.

### 5. Sub-Timeline Governance
All sub-timelines are derivatives of the master chronology and must maintain full traceability.
*   **Source Inheritance:** Every event on a sub-timeline inherits its source attribution and confidence rating from the master chronology. No event may appear on a sub-timeline without a corresponding entry in the master.
*   **Gap Propagation:** Temporal gaps identified in Task 9.2 are carried into the relevant sub-timelines. A financial timeline must show the same gap in transaction records that the master chronology identifies.
*   **Cross-Reference:** Sub-timelines must be cross-referenceable — an event on a communication timeline must link to the same event's entry on the master chronology and, where relevant, to the corresponding entry on a financial or subject-specific timeline.
