Phase 1 of the investigative workflow, termed **Operational Direction & Strategic Foundation**, serves as the "Direction" stage of the Intelligence Cycle. It is designed to ensure that the investigation is conducted with professional rigour, objective reasoning, and auditable logic from the outset. This phase is not merely administrative; it is a defensive and analytical bulwark against "scope creep" and legal challenges, ensuring every action taken is proportionate and necessary.

### Step 1: Defining Operational Goals and Justification
The investigation begins with the deployment of the **Case Decision & Collection Log (CDL) Template**. This is a contemporaneous record that captures the "state of mind" of the investigator and the reasoning behind every strategic choice.

**Task 1.1: Situation and Circumstance Scoping**
Before identifying gaps, the investigator must record the current known situation and define the specific aims of the inquiry. This involves:
*   **Defining Operational Goals:** Clearly articulating what success looks like (e.g., "Identify the ultimate beneficial owner of Entity X").
*   **Gap Analysis:** Specifically identifying what is currently unknown—the "knowledge gaps"—that prevent the operational goals from being met.

**Task 1.2: Environmental Assessment (STEEPLES Framework)**
The investigator must evaluate the environment in which the investigation operates to identify influencing factors. The **STEEPLES** mnemonic is applied:
*   **Social:** Cultural norms or demographic factors affecting the subject.
*   **Technical:** Digital infrastructure, encryption, or technical barriers.
*   **Economic:** Financial motives, market conditions, or economic history.
*   **Environmental:** Physical or geographic constraints.
*   **Political:** Geopolitical implications or state-level interests.
*   **Legal:** Relevant jurisdictions, laws, and regulations.
*   **Ethical:** Privacy considerations and moral boundaries.
*   **Safety:** Risks to the investigator, sources, or the public.

**Task 1.3: Legal and Ethical Justification (PLAN Framework)**
Every investigative vector must be justified. The **PLAN** framework is used to evaluate the preferred options against four criteria:
*   **Proportionality:** Is the proposed intrusion into privacy balanced against the seriousness of the matter?
*   **Legality:** Is there a clear legal basis for this action (e.g., GDPR compliance, CPIA)?
*   **Accountability:** Who is responsible for the decision, and is it documented for audit?
*   **Necessity:** Is this action required to achieve the goal, or is there a less intrusive way?

**Task 1.4: Risk Management and Policy Documentation**
The investigator must record risk management strategies and any "negative decisions"—conscious choices *not* to pursue a specific line of inquiry. Documenting why a lead was abandoned is critical for proving that all reasonable lines of enquiry were pursued.

### Step 2: Strategic Planning and Resource Allocation
Once the foundation is justified, the investigator transitions to the **Investigation Plan / Strategy Document**. This is a dynamic, living document that dictates the cadence and scope of the investigation.

**Task 2.1: Defining Intelligence Requirements (IRs)**
The broad operational goals are broken down into specific, answerable questions. These IRs define exactly what information must be collected to bridge the identified knowledge gaps.

**Task 2.2: Establishing the Intelligence Collection Plan (ICP)**
The ICP details the strategy for gathering information. It involves:
*   **Parallel Sourcing Strategy:** Mandating the exploration of both open and closed sources to prevent single-source reliance.
*   **Sequence of Collection:** Defining whether to start with official government records (Tier 1) or move to verified commercial databases (Tier 2).
*   **Digital Chain of Custody Standards:** Establishing the "evidence vault" requirements, such as the use of cryptographic hashing and automated URL archiving (e.g., via Hunchly).

**Task 2.3: Prioritizing Lines of Enquiry**
All potential leads are ranked. This ensures that resources are first directed toward the most "diagnostic" or high-impact areas of the investigation.

**Task 2.4: Resource Allocation and Tasking**
The plan allocates specific tasks to investigators or automated systems. Each task is recorded in the **Task Log / Actions Register** with a unique reference number, description, and deadline.

**Task 2.5: Establishing the Operational Ontology**
Crucially, before any data extraction begins, the investigator must define the "ontology"—the specific schema (such as POLE: Person, Object, Location, Event) that will be used. This ensures that all data collected later in the process can be resolved into a structured database without the need for costly retrospective changes.

### Administrative and Quality Checkpoints
Before Phase 1 is concluded and "Collection" begins, several administrative tasks must be finalized to ensure the audit trail is unassailable.

*   **Task 3.1: Case Summary Record Initiation:** Create a high-level administrative overview including the case reference number, title, and initial scope statement.
*   **Task 3.2: Defining Terms of Reference (ToR):** Explicitly stating the legal or ethical mandate of the investigation to prevent scope creep.
*   **Task 3.3: Supervisory Review:** In professional contexts, the initial plan must be reviewed by a supervisor or commander within 24–96 hours to certify that the strategy is sound and the justifications (PLAN/STEEPLES) are valid.
*   **Task 3.4: Meta-Log Setup:** Configure the audit trail to mimic digital chain-of-custody requirements, ensuring that every simulated "URL visited" or "source queried" will be recorded with its associated hash values.
