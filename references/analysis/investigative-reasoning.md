# Investigative Reasoning

Techniques for thinking during active investigation. Load this reference when testing causal claims, encountering contradictions, assessing narratives, or mapping systemic patterns in UK drill cases.

---

## Narrative Causation Scoring

When a causal claim surfaces during investigation — from community accounts, Reddit threads, drill lyrics, news reports, or court documents — score it across 6 dimensions before accepting it.

Adapted from Skinner's superstitious conditioning research (1948) and Timberlake & Lucas (1985).

### The 6 Dimensions

| Dimension | High Pigeon Risk (weak causation) | Low Pigeon Risk (strong causation) |
|-----------|----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Temporal Contiguity** | Alleged cause immediately preceded effect with no mechanism specified | Cause and effect connected by a chain with known steps and latency |
| **Confound Density** | Multiple variables changed simultaneously (estate changes, personnel shifts, drug market disruption, policy changes) | Only the alleged cause changed in the relevant window |
| **Base Rate** | This type of effect occurs frequently without the alleged cause (e.g. beefs start regularly without diss tracks) | This type of effect is rare without the alleged cause |
| **Mechanism Specificity** | No plausible mechanism beyond "it just happened" or "everyone knows" | Specific, traceable chain connecting cause to effect |
| **Replication** | Claimed pattern observed once or in this case only | Pattern observed across multiple independent cases |
| **Reversibility** | Removing the alleged cause has never been tested or observed | Removing the cause reliably removed the effect in comparable situations |

### Scoring

Each dimension scored: **causal** / **uncertain** / **pigeon**

- 5-6 causal: Strong evidence for the claimed causation. Likely real.
- 3-4 causal: Partially grounded. Worth investigating further but hold lightly.
- 1-2 causal: Substantially pigeon. The community narrative is probably wrong about WHY, even if they're right about WHAT happened.
- 0 causal: Full pigeon. Maintained entirely by repetition, not evidence.

### Investigation Application

Common drill pigeon patterns:

| Narrative Pattern | Pigeon Risk | Why |
|------------------|-------------|-----|
| "The beef started because of the diss track" | HIGH | Temporal contiguity only. Confound density high (pre-existing tensions). Base rate: most diss tracks do NOT start beefs. |
| "He got stabbed because he was lacking" | HIGH | Post-hoc rationalisation. Mechanism is circular ("he was vulnerable so he got attacked"). |
| "The gang formed after X incident" | MEDIUM | May be true origin, or may be retroactive narrative imposed on gradual social clustering. Check: did the group exist informally before the incident? |
| "County lines started because of social media" | HIGH | Confound density extreme. Drug distribution economics, austerity, policing changes all moved simultaneously. |

### Entanglement

Causal claims that share evidence or were formed in the same context are entangled. Collapsing one affects others.

Detection: Two claims are entangled when they share supporting evidence (the same Reddit thread supports both), were formed in the same period, or form a narrative chain ("X led to Y which caused Z").

Before challenging any narrative claim, map its entanglements. Collapsing "the beef started from the diss track" may cascade into "drill music causes violence" which cascades into "banning drill reduces crime." Map the chain FIRST.

## Contradiction Holding

When evidence from different sources contradicts — court documents say one thing, community accounts say another, drill lyrics imply a third — hold the contradiction productively instead of collapsing to one side.

### The Protocol

**Step 1: Name both poles explicitly.**
```
POLE A: [First version, stated as if absolutely true]
POLE B: [Second version, stated as if absolutely true]  
COLLISION: [Where they cannot both be true]
```

**Step 2: Audit avoidance.**
How are you trying to escape the tension?
- Collapsing to the "official" version (court documents) because it feels authoritative
- Collapsing to the community version because it's more detailed
- Averaging ("the truth is somewhere in the middle") — this is lazy, sometimes one pole is simply right
- Compartmentalising (treating them as separate topics to avoid the collision)

**Step 3: Classify the contradiction.**

| Type | Definition | Investigation Response |
|------|-----------|----------------------|
| True paradox | Both genuinely cannot be true | One source is wrong. Which one and why? |
| False dichotomy | Appears contradictory but both can be true in different frames | Find the frame difference (different timeframes, different perspectives on same events) |
| Context-dependent | True in different contexts | Map which context each applies to |
| Scale-dependent | True at different scales (individual vs systemic) | Both may be needed for the full picture |
| Temporal | True at different times | Reconstruct the timeline to see when each was true |

**Step 4: Extract what the collision reveals.**
The most important question: what would be invisible if you resolved this prematurely?

## Cause-Effect Verification

Before accepting any causal explanation in an investigation, apply the Symptom Test.

### The Symptom Test (5 Questions)

| Question | If YES: Likely Symptom | If NO: Potential Root Cause |
|----------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| Is this a direct observation of distress? | "Three people got stabbed" | "The estate's youth services were defunded in 2012" |
| Can the effect be made to go away by overriding the system? | "Increased police presence reduced incidents temporarily" | "Community-led intervention changed the underlying dynamic" |
| Does the problem recur when intervention stops? | "Violence returned when police left" | "The structural change held" |
| Is the explanation circular? | "They stabbed him because there's a beef" | "They stabbed him because territorial drug competition escalated after X" |
| Does it blame individual character? | "He was a bad kid" | "The system produced this outcome through these specific mechanisms" |

3+ YES answers: you are looking at a symptom. Dig deeper for the structural cause.

### The Therefore Test

Reverse the causal chain. If A caused B, then: "A happened, therefore B happened." Does the chain survive?

Then test: "If A had NOT happened, would B still have happened?" If yes, A is not the cause. Something else drove B and A was coincidental.

## Temporal Analysis

When building timelines, check for temporal blindness.

### The 4 Blindness Types

| Type | Definition | Test Question |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **Order Blindness** | Assuming sequence doesn't matter | "What changes if events happened in reverse order?" |
| **Horizon Blindness** | Treating the future as not real | "What's being deferred that will compound?" |
| **Causality Blindness** | Confusing sequence with causation | "Which actually came first? How do we know, not just believe?" |
| **Lock-in Blindness** | Missing points of no return | "After what point could this not have been reversed?" |

### Investigation Application

Reconstruct timelines BEFORE forming hypotheses about causation. The UNESCO manual's chronological scan: search "[subject] [date]" for each relevant period. Build the timeline from raw dates first. Only THEN look for patterns.

The most common temporal error in drill investigation: assuming the incident that gets mentioned most is the origin, when the actual origin predates it by months or years.

## Perspective Mapping

When modelling how different parties experience the same events.

### For Each Stakeholder (gang member, family, estate resident, police, council, victim, artist)

Map:
1. **What they see** — what information is available to them
2. **What they optimise for** — survival, status, money, safety, reputation, justice
3. **What they fear** — what they're trying to avoid
4. **What they cannot see** — structural blindspots from their position

Model their internal logic as coherent. If a gang member's behaviour seems irrational, the model is incomplete. Find what makes it rational from their position.

## Systems Mapping

When the investigation reveals systemic patterns (estate decline, policy failure, drug economics, generational cycles).

### Feedback Loop Identification

For any recurring pattern, identify:

**Reinforcing loops (R):** Dynamics that amplify themselves.
Example: Youth excluded from school → more time on estate → deeper gang involvement → more exclusions.

**Balancing loops (B):** Dynamics that resist change.
Example: Police crackdown → visible activity moves indoors → statistics improve → funding cut → activity returns.

**Time delays:** Where the effect of a cause takes months or years to manifest.
Example: Youth service defunding in 2012 → visible impact on gang recruitment in 2015-2017.

### Leverage Points

Where could a small change produce disproportionate systemic impact? Adapted from Meadows (1999).

In drill investigation, the highest-leverage findings are usually:
- Changes to rules and incentive structures (sentencing policy changes, housing allocation policy)
- Information flow changes (social media enabling recruitment, drill music as communication channel)
- System goals (what the estate was designed to achieve vs what it actually optimises for)

The lowest-leverage findings (but the most commonly reported) are:
- Individual incidents
- Individual actors
- Parameter changes (more police, more cameras)

## Inversion

When stuck on a hypothesis, invert it.

"The beef caused the violence" → "The violence caused the beef" (violence happened first, the beef narrative was constructed afterwards to make sense of it).

"Drill music incites violence" → "Violence incites drill music" (the music documents and processes violence that already exists).

"The gang formed and then controlled the territory" → "The territory conditions produced the group, which was then labelled a gang."

For each inversion, explore at least 3 implications before dismissing. The inverted version is often closer to the truth than the original.

## Meta-Pattern Recognition

When the same dynamic appears across multiple investigations or cases.

A pattern is universal when:
1. It appears in 3+ genuinely distinct cases (different postcodes, different groups, different time periods)
2. The mechanism driving it is identifiable (not just surface similarity)
3. It makes predictions in new cases that can be tested

When a meta-pattern is identified, it becomes part of the detective journal — career learning that applies to future investigations.
