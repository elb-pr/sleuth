Task 12.2, **Assessing Diagnosticity and Matrix Scoring**, is the most cognitively demanding step in the ACH process. For every cell in the matrix — every intersection of evidence and hypothesis — the analyst must ask a specific, carefully framed question: "If this hypothesis is true, how likely is it that I would see this evidence?"

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. The Diagnosticity Question
The analyst works through the matrix cell by cell, evaluating each piece of evidence against each hypothesis independently.
*   **The Key Question:** "Given that Hypothesis X is true, is this evidence Consistent, Inconsistent, or Not Applicable?" This is not the same as asking "Does this evidence support Hypothesis X?" — the latter invites confirmation bias. The former forces the analyst to reason from the hypothesis outward to the evidence, rather than from the evidence toward a preferred conclusion.
*   **Independence of Assessment:** Each cell is scored independently. The analyst must resist the urge to score an entire row at once (which creates anchoring effects) or to let the scoring of one hypothesis influence the scoring of another.

### 2. Scoring Conventions
Each cell receives one of five ratings:
*   **CC (Very Consistent):** If this hypothesis is true, the evidence is strongly expected — it would be surprising not to see it.
*   **C (Consistent):** If this hypothesis is true, the evidence is plausible — it fits comfortably within the explanatory framework.
*   **N/A (Not Applicable):** The evidence has no diagnostic relationship to this hypothesis — it neither supports nor contradicts it.
*   **I (Inconsistent):** If this hypothesis is true, the evidence would be unexpected — it does not fit the explanatory framework.
*   **II (Very Inconsistent):** If this hypothesis is true, the evidence is strongly contradictory — it would be very surprising to see it.

### 3. Pruning the Matrix
After the initial scoring pass, the analyst reviews the matrix for evidence items that lack diagnostic value.
*   **Universal Consistency:** If a piece of evidence is rated C or CC against every hypothesis, it cannot discriminate between them. While it may be a true fact, it does not help determine which hypothesis is correct. These items are flagged and may be removed from the active matrix to prevent them from skewing the visual impression.
*   **Universal N/A:** Evidence rated N/A against every hypothesis is irrelevant to the analytical question and should be removed.
*   **Retained for Record:** Pruned items are not deleted — they are moved to an appendix of the ACH record. The pruning decision and rationale are documented so that a reviewer can verify no relevant evidence was discarded.

### 4. Cognitive Bias Safeguards
The scoring process is the point at which cognitive biases are most likely to distort the analysis. Several safeguards are applied:
*   **Column-by-Column Review:** After the initial cell-by-cell scoring, the analyst reviews each column (hypothesis) holistically. Does the pattern of ratings make internal sense? Is there a logical coherence to the consistency and inconsistency ratings for this hypothesis?
*   **Peer Review:** In multi-analyst teams, the scored matrix should be reviewed by a second analyst who independently scores a subset of cells and compares results. Significant disagreements trigger discussion and resolution.
*   **Anchoring Prevention:** The analyst should avoid scoring their favoured hypothesis first, as the initial scoring creates an anchor that biases subsequent assessments. Best practice is to begin with the hypothesis considered least likely.

### 5. Source Weighting Considerations
Not all evidence items carry equal weight, but the standard ACH methodology treats each row as equally important in the initial scoring.
*   **Admiralty Grade Annotation:** Each evidence item carries its Admiralty grade visibly in the matrix. A piece of evidence graded "A1" (completely reliable source, confirmed information) should carry more analytical weight than one graded "F5" (unverifiable source, improbable information).
*   **Weighting in Practice:** Some ACH implementations apply numeric weights to evidence based on source reliability and credibility. This produces a weighted inconsistency score in Task 12.3 rather than a simple count. The choice of weighting methodology must be documented and consistently applied.
*   **Deferred to Sensitivity Analysis:** The formal impact of source quality on the final conclusion is rigorously tested in the Sensitivity Analysis (Task 12.3), where the analyst explicitly examines what happens when low-quality evidence is removed from the matrix.
