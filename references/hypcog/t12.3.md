Task 12.3, **Quantitative Analysis and Drawing Conclusions**, moves from cell-level scoring to hypothesis-level ranking. The method is mathematical rather than intuitive — the analyst calculates inconsistency scores and identifies the surviving hypothesis based on refutation rather than confirmation.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Calculating Inconsistency Scores
The analyst tallies the inconsistency ratings for each hypothesis (each column in the matrix).
*   **Simple Count:** In the basic ACH methodology, the analyst counts the number of "I" (Inconsistent) and "II" (Very Inconsistent) ratings for each hypothesis. "II" ratings may be double-weighted to reflect their greater diagnostic severity.
*   **Weighted Count:** In weighted implementations, each inconsistency rating is multiplied by the evidence item's reliability weight (derived from its Admiralty grade). A strong inconsistency from a high-reliability source (e.g., "A1" evidence rated "II") carries more weight than an inconsistency from a low-reliability source (e.g., "F5" evidence rated "I").
*   **Tabulation:** The inconsistency scores are presented in a summary row at the bottom of the matrix, enabling direct comparison across hypotheses.

### 2. Identifying the Most Likely Explanation
The Inconsistency Principle dictates that the most likely hypothesis is the one with the lowest inconsistency score — the explanation that the available evidence has the hardest time disproving.
*   **Not the Most Confirmed:** This is the critical conceptual distinction of ACH. The winning hypothesis is not the one with the most "CC" and "C" ratings — it is the one with the fewest "I" and "II" ratings. A hypothesis that is consistent with all evidence but inconsistent with none may be correct, but a hypothesis with more confirmations but also significant inconsistencies is weaker.
*   **Clear Winner vs Close Contest:** If one hypothesis has a significantly lower inconsistency score than all others, the conclusion is relatively confident. If two or more hypotheses have similar inconsistency scores, the analyst must acknowledge the ambiguity — the evidence base does not clearly discriminate between them, and additional collection may be required.
*   **Rejection Documentation:** Hypotheses that are rejected (high inconsistency scores) must have their rejection explicitly documented with the specific evidence items that drove the rejection. This documentation is essential for the final briefing (Phase 6) and for legal disclosure.

### 3. Sensitivity Analysis
The analyst tests the robustness of the conclusion by examining its dependency on specific evidence items.
*   **Critical Evidence Identification:** Which specific pieces of evidence, if removed, would change the ranking of hypotheses? These are the items on which the entire conclusion depends.
*   **Source Reliability Test:** The analyst asks: "What if the evidence items graded 'F' or '5/6' on the Admiralty scale are wrong, deceptive, or based on an unreliable source?" If removing these low-reliability items changes the winning hypothesis, the conclusion is fragile and must be caveated accordingly.
*   **Assumption Vulnerability:** The analyst identifies which assumptions (labelled separately in Task 12.1) are most critical. If the conclusion depends on an assumption that has not been verified, this fragility is reported.
*   **Single-Source Dependency:** If the conclusion rests primarily on evidence from a single source, this dependency is flagged. The Parallel Sourcing mandate from Phase 2 (Task 3.2b) should have mitigated this, but the Sensitivity Analysis provides the final check.

### 4. Documenting the Analytical Conclusion
The conclusion is derived logically from the matrix scores and sensitivity analysis, not from the analyst's intuition.
*   **Conclusion Statement:** A structured statement identifying the most likely hypothesis, the inconsistency scores that support this determination, the key evidence items that differentiate it from competing hypotheses, and the specific evidence items and assumptions on which the conclusion is most dependent.
*   **Confidence Level:** The analyst assigns an **ICD 203 confidence level** (High, Moderate, or Low) to the conclusion based on the quality and diversity of the underlying evidence. High confidence requires high-quality information and sound reasoning. Low confidence reflects significant gaps, questionable reliability, or fragile assumptions.
*   **Likelihood Expression:** The probability of the leading hypothesis is expressed using **ICD 203 probability language** — "almost certainly" (95–99%), "very likely" (80–95%), "likely" (55–80%), "roughly even chance" (45–55%), etc. Confidence and likelihood must never appear in the same sentence to prevent reader confusion between the two dimensions.

### 5. Output for Final Briefing
The ACH analysis produces three deliverables for Phase 6:
*   **The Scored Matrix:** The complete matrix with all ratings, inconsistency scores, and pruning annotations.
*   **The Sensitivity Analysis Summary:** A narrative identifying the critical evidence items, fragile assumptions, and source dependencies.
*   **The Analytical Conclusion:** The structured conclusion with ICD 203 confidence and likelihood expressions, ready for integration into the Analytical Briefing (Task 13.1).
