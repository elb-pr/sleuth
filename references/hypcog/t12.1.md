Task 12.1, **Hypothesis Generation and Matrix Setup**, defines the parameters of the **Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)** exercise before any scoring begins. ACH, pioneered by Richards J. Heuer Jr. at the CIA's Center for the Study of Intelligence, operates on the scientific principle of refutation rather than confirmation. The analyst does not seek to prove a favoured theory — instead, the method identifies which hypothesis the available evidence has the hardest time disproving.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Hypothesis Identification
The analyst must brainstorm all plausible explanations for the investigative problem, without premature filtering or judgment.
*   **Mutual Exclusivity:** Each hypothesis must be distinct from every other — if Hypothesis A is true, Hypothesis B cannot also be true. This prevents overlapping explanations from diluting the matrix's discriminatory power.
*   **Collective Exhaustiveness:** The set of hypotheses should cover all reasonable possibilities. At minimum, three hypotheses are required to prevent "satisficing" — the cognitive tendency to stop reasoning as soon as a seemingly adequate explanation is found. Ideally, the set includes at least one hypothesis that the analyst considers unlikely, to force genuine consideration of alternatives.
*   **The "Devil's Advocate" Hypothesis:** Best practice includes at least one hypothesis that directly contradicts the analyst's initial instinct or the prevailing institutional assumption. This forces structured consideration of the alternative explanation, combating confirmation bias at the design stage.

### 2. Establishing the Evidence List (Y-Axis)
The analyst assembles a comprehensive list of all significant evidence, facts, and assumptions that will be evaluated against the hypotheses.
*   **Evidence Sources:** The Y-axis draws from the entire investigative output of Phases 1 through 4:
    *   Entity Register records and relationship data (Phase 3).
    *   Chronological Matrix findings — temporal patterns, sequences, and gaps (Phase 4, Step 9).
    *   Network Architecture outputs — centrality analysis results, cluster structures, two-mode projections (Phase 4, Step 10).
    *   Content Verification Reports — authenticated spatial and temporal evidence (Phase 4, Step 11).
    *   Subject and Corporate Profiles — risk indicators, ownership structures, financial anomalies (Phase 3, Task 8.1).
*   **Inclusion Criteria:** Evidence is included if it has diagnostic potential — if it could, in principle, differentiate between at least two hypotheses. Evidence that is equally consistent with all hypotheses has no diagnostic value and should be excluded to prevent visual clutter.
*   **Granularity:** Each evidence item should represent a single, discrete fact or finding. Compound evidence items (e.g., "Entity A has links to multiple shell companies and unusual financial patterns") must be decomposed into individual facts so that each can be independently assessed.

### 3. Explicitly Identifying Assumptions
Assumptions are not evidence — they are suppositions used to fill knowledge gaps or frame arguments. They must be formally separated from facts.
*   **Assumption Documentation:** Each assumption is listed alongside the evidence but explicitly labelled as an assumption. For example, "Assuming that Entity A's travel to Jurisdiction X was for business purposes" is an assumption, not a verified fact.
*   **Assumption Sensitivity:** Assumptions are particularly vulnerable in the Sensitivity Analysis (Task 12.3). If the final conclusion depends heavily on an assumption that could be wrong, the conclusion is fragile.

### 4. Matrix Construction
The hypotheses (X-axis) and evidence items (Y-axis) are plotted into a structured matrix grid.
*   **Physical Layout:** Each column represents a hypothesis. Each row represents a piece of evidence or an assumption. Each cell will receive a consistency rating in Task 12.2.
*   **Source Metadata Attachment:** Each evidence item on the Y-axis carries its Admiralty grade (from Phase 2, Task 3.1b) and its Source Register reference. This metadata is essential for the Sensitivity Analysis in Task 12.3, where the analyst tests whether conclusions change if low-reliability evidence is removed.
*   **Empty Matrix Review:** Before scoring begins, the analyst reviews the matrix structure to verify: are the hypotheses genuinely mutually exclusive? Is the evidence list comprehensive? Are all assumptions labelled? Is each evidence item granular enough for independent assessment?
