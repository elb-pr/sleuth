Task 4.2, **Mandatory Recording of "Negative Results"**, is the procedural requirement to document failed or null enquiries with the same rigour as successful ones. In investigative methodology, what was searched and not found is as analytically significant as what was found. This task is the primary defence against accusations of **narrative bias** — the selective presentation of only those facts that support a preferred conclusion.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Definition and Scope of a Negative Result
A negative result is any investigative action that does not yield the expected or sought information. This includes but is not limited to:
*   **Database Searches Returning No Records:** Querying a company registry, land registry, or sanctions list and finding no entries matching the subject.
*   **Sources That Cannot Be Located:** Attempting to contact a human source, access a historical record, or retrieve a web page that no longer exists.
*   **Sources That Refuse to Cooperate:** A witness or institution declining to provide information (where legally entitled to do so).
*   **Technical Verification Failures:** Media that cannot be geolocated, chronolocated, or authenticated through the methods in Task 3.3b.
*   **Leads That Do Not Develop:** An initial hypothesis or line of enquiry that, upon investigation, produces no supporting evidence.

### 2. Documentation Standards
Every negative result must be recorded in the **Task Log / Actions Register** with the same mandatory fields as a positive finding:
*   **Action Reference Number:** Linking to the original tasking entry.
*   **Source Queried:** Exactly which database, registry, platform, or individual was consulted.
*   **Search Parameters Used:** The precise query terms, date ranges, jurisdictions, or identifiers employed. This enables a third party to reproduce the search and verify the null result.
*   **Date and Time of Search:** UTC-normalised timestamp confirming when the search was executed, which is critical because databases update continuously — a negative result today may become a positive result tomorrow.
*   **Result:** An explicit statement that the search produced no relevant records, with any partial or ambiguous results described in full.
*   **Analyst Assessment:** A brief note explaining whether the negative result is analytically significant (e.g., "The absence of any property holdings in Jurisdiction X is inconsistent with the subject's claimed net worth") or expected (e.g., "No records found; this registry does not cover the relevant time period").

### 3. Legal and Disclosure Significance
In law enforcement and regulatory contexts, negative results are not administrative housekeeping — they are legally mandated disclosable material.
*   **UK CPIA Disclosure:** Under the Criminal Procedure and Investigations Act 1996, the prosecution must disclose material that "might reasonably be considered capable of undermining the case for the prosecution" or "assisting the case for the accused." Negative results — evidence that was sought and not found — frequently fall into this category. Failure to record and disclose them is a prosecutorial failure.
*   **UK MG5 Standard:** The case summary must remain objective and balanced. Negative results demonstrating that alternative explanations were investigated and found unsupported are essential to meeting this standard.
*   **"All Reasonable Lines of Enquiry":** The legal requirement to pursue all reasonable lines of enquiry means that the investigator must demonstrate not only what was investigated but also what was investigated and yielded nothing. Without negative result documentation, the investigation cannot prove it was thorough.

### 4. Analytical Significance
Beyond legal compliance, negative results serve critical analytical functions within the investigative workflow:
*   **Gap Confirmation:** A negative result may confirm that a knowledge gap identified in the **Intelligence Collection Plan** (Task 2.2) genuinely cannot be filled with available sources, triggering a re-evaluation of the collection strategy.
*   **Hypothesis Testing (Phase 5):** In the **ACH Matrix**, the absence of expected evidence is itself diagnostic. If Hypothesis A predicts that Entity X should hold assets in Jurisdiction Y, and a thorough search of Jurisdiction Y's registries reveals nothing, this absence is scored as "Inconsistent" with Hypothesis A — potentially altering the final analytical conclusion.
*   **Deception Indicators:** Systematic negative results across multiple expected data points (e.g., a corporation with no employees, no tax filings, and no physical premises) may indicate the entity is a shell or front, which becomes a finding in its own right.

### 5. Preventing the "File Drawer" Problem
The term "file drawer problem" originates in academic research, where studies with null results are disproportionately unpublished, distorting the evidence base. In investigative work, the equivalent failure is burying negative results because they do not advance the narrative.
*   **Equal Prominence:** Negative results must be recorded with the same structural prominence as positive findings in the Task Log. They must not be relegated to informal notes, verbal briefings, or omitted entirely.
*   **Supervisory Audit:** During the Phase 2 Administrative Checkpoint, the supervisor must verify that the ratio of positive to negative results is plausible. An investigation with exclusively positive findings and no documented negative results is a red flag for confirmation bias.
