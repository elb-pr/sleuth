Task 3.1b, **Application of the Admiralty 6x6 Matrix**, is the epistemic gateway of Phase 2. Every discrete piece of information entering the investigation must pass through this bipartite evaluation before it can be ingested into the **Entity Register** or used as evidence in the **ACH Matrix**. The system is formally codified in **AJP-2.1 (Allied Joint Doctrine for Intelligence Procedures), Edition B (2016)** under **STANAG 2511 (2003)** and represents the dominant source evaluation framework across Western intelligence, law enforcement, and corporate intelligence contexts.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Independent Assessment of Source Reliability (A–F)
The first axis evaluates the provider's historical track record, wholly independent of the current information being supplied.
*   **A (Completely Reliable):** No doubt about the source's authenticity, trustworthiness, or competency; history of complete reliability.
*   **B (Usually Reliable):** Minor doubts; strong overall track record.
*   **C (Fairly Reliable):** Genuine doubt exists; some valid information provided in the past.
*   **D (Not Usually Reliable):** Significant doubt; occasional valid information.
*   **E (Unreliable):** History of providing invalid information; lacks authenticity, trustworthiness, competency.
*   **F (Reliability Cannot Be Judged):** No established track record; default for new sources. This is the most common rating in OSINT work, where an untested digital source has no verifiable history.

### 2. Independent Assessment of Information Credibility (1–6)
The second axis evaluates the data's internal logic and external corroboration, assessed entirely separately from the source rating.
*   **1 (Confirmed):** Independently corroborated by other sources; logically sound; consistent with other reporting.
*   **2 (Probably True):** Not independently confirmed; logically sound; consistent with other information.
*   **3 (Possibly True):** Not confirmed; reasonably logical; partially consistent.
*   **4 (Doubtful):** Possible but not logical; no other information to compare.
*   **5 (Improbable):** Not confirmed; illogical; actively contradicted by other reporting.
*   **6 (Truth Cannot Be Judged):** Insufficient basis for evaluation.

### 3. Preventing Cognitive Conflation
The most critical procedural requirement is that the two axes remain strictly independent. The framework explicitly prohibits two common analytical errors:
*   **Halo Effect:** An analyst must not rate a source as "A" simply because the information appears highly plausible. The source's history is assessed on its own merits, regardless of the current claim.
*   **Authority Bias:** Conversely, an analyst must not rate information as "1" simply because it originates from a historically reliable source. A completely reliable source can still transmit false or unverifiable data.
*   **Output Format:** The combined alphanumeric grade (e.g., "B2" indicating a Usually Reliable source providing Probably True information) is appended to the intelligence record in the **Source Register** and carried forward as metadata through the entire analytical chain.

### 4. The OSINT Mismatch Problem
Traditional Admiralty grading was designed for human intelligence networks with established track records. In OSINT contexts, a critical methodological friction arises:
*   **The Default F6 Problem:** An anonymous social media account posting a verifiable, geolocatable video would receive an "F6" rating under orthodox application — effectively marking it as worthless intelligence despite undeniable physical proof.
*   **Mitigation — Digital Verification Override:** Modern OSINT practice pivots heavily toward data validity over source history. When a source defaults to "F", the credibility axis must be assessed through technical verification methods (reverse image search, geolocation, chronolocation) as defined in Task 3.3b. If the data can be technically corroborated to "1" or "2", the combined "F1" or "F2" rating correctly communicates: untested source, but confirmed information.

### 5. The UK Law Enforcement Extension (5×5×5 System)
In UK policing contexts, the Admiralty system is extended with a third dimension: a **handling code** (1–5) specifying who may receive the information and what they may do with it. This produces three-digit ratings (e.g., 2/3/2) covering source reliability, intelligence reliability, and handling restriction simultaneously. The 5×5×5 system is mandatory for intelligence recorded on police systems in England and Wales under College of Policing guidance.

### 6. Integration with Downstream Processes
The Admiralty grade assigned at this stage is not a one-time label; it propagates through the entire investigative workflow:
*   **Entity Register (Phase 3):** Every record ingested carries its source grade as mandatory metadata, ensuring the database is never "poisoned" by unvetted data.
*   **ACH Matrix (Phase 5):** When evidence is loaded onto the Y-axis of the reasoning matrix, its Admiralty grade directly informs the **Sensitivity Analysis** — the analyst must ask whether conclusions would change if evidence graded "F" or "5/6" were removed.
*   **Final Briefing (Phase 6):** The **Mandatory Source Summary Statement** aggregates the quality of the underlying source base, drawing directly from the grades assigned here.
