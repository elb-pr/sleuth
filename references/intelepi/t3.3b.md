Task 3.3b, **OSINT Technical Verification**, is the digital counterpart to traditional source vetting. Where the Admiralty Matrix (Task 3.1b) evaluates human-source reliability through track record, this task evaluates digital media through technical forensics. It is executed using the **Content Verification Report** and is the primary mechanism for resolving the "OSINT Mismatch" — the problem of anonymous digital sources defaulting to "F" on the reliability axis despite carrying technically verifiable data.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Reverse Image and Video Search
The first verification step determines whether the media is original or has been repurposed from a different context.
*   **Process:** The investigator submits the image or video keyframes to reverse search engines (e.g., Google Reverse Image Search, TinEye, Yandex Images) to identify prior appearances.
*   **Analytical Purpose:** If the media appears in an earlier context — for example, a photograph purportedly from a current event is found in a news article from three years prior — the content is flagged as potentially misleading or deliberately deceptive. The original context is recorded in the **Source Register** (Task 5.3).
*   **Negative Result Value:** If no prior appearances are found, this is documented as supporting (but not confirming) the claim that the media is original. The absence of prior results is itself a data point.

### 2. Geolocation
The second verification step establishes the physical location depicted in the media by matching visual cues to known geography.
*   **Visual Cue Analysis:** The investigator identifies distinctive features within the image or video — street signs, building architecture, vegetation types, terrain features, vehicle registration plates, or commercial signage — and cross-references these against satellite imagery (e.g., Google Earth, Sentinel Hub) and street-level photography (e.g., Google Street View, Mapillary).
*   **Coordinate Confirmation:** When sufficient visual cues converge on a single location, the investigator records the latitude/longitude coordinates and the confidence level of the match. A match based on a single ambiguous cue (e.g., a generic building style) is rated lower than a match based on multiple unique identifiers (e.g., a specific intersection with identifiable shop fronts).
*   **Geolocation Failure Documentation:** If the media cannot be geolocated, this is recorded as a knowledge gap in the **Intelligence Collection Plan** and may trigger additional collection tasking.

### 3. Chronolocation
The third verification step determines when the media was captured, independent of any metadata claims.
*   **Shadow Analysis (SunCalc):** By measuring the direction and length of shadows in an image and combining this with the geolocated coordinates, the investigator can calculate the approximate time and date of capture using solar position tools (e.g., SunCalc, pysolar). This technique is most effective in open areas with clearly defined shadows and known object heights.
*   **Weather Cross-Reference:** The weather conditions visible in the media (cloud cover, precipitation, snow coverage, wet surfaces) are compared against historical weather records for the geolocated area (e.g., Open-Meteo historical API, Weather Underground archives). Inconsistencies between claimed dates and observed weather are flagged.
*   **Vegetation and Seasonal Analysis:** In rural or semi-rural settings, the state of vegetation (leaf coverage, crop growth stages, flowering patterns) can narrow the temporal window to a specific season or month.
*   **Temporal Markers:** Any visible date-specific content — newspaper front pages, event posters, seasonal decorations, construction progress on known projects — provides hard chronological anchors.

### 4. Metadata Extraction and Verification
Where available, the embedded metadata of digital files is extracted and assessed for consistency with the technical findings.
*   **EXIF Data Analysis:** Digital photographs may contain EXIF metadata recording camera model, GPS coordinates, date/time of capture, and exposure settings. This data is extracted using forensic tools (e.g., ExifTool, PyExifTool) and compared against the geolocation and chronolocation findings.
*   **Metadata Stripping Detection:** Many social media platforms strip EXIF data on upload. The absence of metadata is not evidence of tampering — it is expected behaviour. However, metadata that is present but inconsistent with the visual evidence (e.g., GPS coordinates that do not match the geolocated scene) is a significant red flag.
*   **File Forensics:** For high-stakes verification, the investigator may examine file compression artefacts, editing history, and format-specific signatures to detect manipulation (e.g., Evidence Level Analysis, or ELA, for detecting composite imagery).

### 5. Output: The Content Verification Report
The combined results of reverse search, geolocation, chronolocation, and metadata analysis are compiled into a **Content Verification Report** that accompanies the media through the investigative chain.
*   **Verification Status:** Each piece of media is classified as **Verified** (technical evidence supports the claimed context), **Partially Verified** (some elements confirmed, others unresolved), **Unverified** (insufficient data to confirm or deny), or **Falsified** (technical evidence contradicts the claimed context).
*   **Admiralty Grade Update:** The verification status directly informs the credibility axis (1–6) of the Admiralty grade assigned in Task 3.1b. A technically verified "F1" rating — untested source, confirmed information — is a legitimate and analytically valuable outcome.
*   **Evidentiary Chain:** The report is linked to the relevant record in the **Evidence Register** (Task 5.1) with its cryptographic hash, ensuring the verification and the verified media are permanently associated.
