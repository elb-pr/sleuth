Task 4.3, **Real-Time Policy Deviation Logging**, is the procedural mechanism for documenting any departure from the original **Investigation Plan** at the moment it occurs. This task exists because investigations are dynamic — new information frequently demands changes in direction, scope, or method. The critical requirement is that these deviations are justified and recorded contemporaneously, not rationalised after the fact.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Definition of a Policy Deviation
A policy deviation is any investigative action or decision that falls outside the boundaries established by the **Intelligence Collection Plan (ICP)**, the **Terms of Reference (ToR)**, or the **PLAN** framework (Proportionality, Legality, Accountability, Necessity) defined in Phase 1.
*   **Scope Expansion:** Investigating an entity, jurisdiction, or line of enquiry not covered by the original ToR. For example, the ICP targets Entity A's UK corporate holdings, but the investigator discovers a link to an offshore entity in a jurisdiction not originally scoped.
*   **Method Change:** Using a collection method not authorised in the ICP. For example, shifting from passive open-source research to direct contact with a subject or third party.
*   **Priority Reordering:** Abandoning or deprioritising a planned line of enquiry in favour of a newly discovered lead that was not anticipated in the original ICP.
*   **Resource Reallocation:** Diverting investigative resources (time, personnel, tool subscriptions) from planned tasks to unplanned activities.

### 2. The Contemporaneity Requirement
The defining principle of deviation logging is that entries must reflect the investigator's "true state of mind" at the time the decision was made.
*   **Real-Time Entry:** The deviation must be logged in the **Case Decision Log (CDL)** at the point the decision is made, not after the new line of enquiry has been explored and its results are known. This prevents hindsight bias — the unconscious tendency to justify past decisions using information that only became available later.
*   **Before-the-Fact Justification:** The log entry must explain why the deviation is necessary based on the information available at the time. The entry should include: the trigger event (what new information prompted the change), the specific deviation from the plan, and the justification under the PLAN framework.
*   **No Post-Hoc Rationalisation:** If an investigator deviates first and documents later, the entry must explicitly acknowledge the timing gap and provide an honest account of the circumstances that prevented contemporaneous logging. Systematic failures to log in real-time undermine the auditability of the entire investigation.

### 3. Structured Deviation Entry Format
Each deviation entry in the CDL follows the same decision matrix used throughout Phase 1:
*   **Situation and Circumstances:** What new information or event triggered the deviation?
*   **Aims and Outcome:** What does the investigator hope to achieve by deviating? How does this serve or modify the original operational goals?
*   **Influencing Factors (STEEPLES):** Are there Social, Technical, Economic, Environmental, Political, Legal, Ethical, or Safety factors specific to the deviation?
*   **Assessment of Choices (PLAN):** Is the deviation Proportionate to the seriousness of the matter? Is it Legal in the relevant jurisdiction? Who is Accountable for authorising it? Is it Necessary, or could the original plan still achieve the objective?
*   **Risk Management:** What risks does the deviation introduce (e.g., scope creep, legal exposure, resource depletion) and how will they be mitigated?

### 4. Authorisation Levels
Not all deviations carry equal risk, and the authorisation requirement scales accordingly:
*   **Minor Deviations:** Changes in the sequence of planned collection (e.g., querying Tier 2 sources before Tier 1 due to access constraints) may be logged by the investigator and reviewed at the next supervisory checkpoint.
*   **Significant Deviations:** Expanding the investigation into a new jurisdiction, entity, or method requires approval from the lead investigator or case supervisor before the new activity is undertaken. The approval is recorded in the CDL.
*   **Fundamental Deviations:** Changes that alter the original Terms of Reference or the legal basis of the investigation (e.g., shifting from civil due diligence to potential criminal referral) require formal re-authorisation, which may involve legal counsel, management, or a commissioning authority.

### 5. Integration with the Broader Audit Trail
Deviation entries do not exist in isolation — they must be cross-referenced across the investigative record:
*   **ICP Update:** Following any approved deviation, the Intelligence Collection Plan must be updated to reflect the new scope, priorities, or methods. The updated ICP references the CDL deviation entry by its unique identifier.
*   **Task Log Link:** New actions arising from the deviation are logged in the Task Log (Task 4.1) with a reference to the authorising CDL entry, creating a traceable chain from decision to action.
*   **Negative Decision Record:** If the deviation involves abandoning a previously planned line of enquiry, the reasons for abandonment are recorded as a "Negative Decision" in the CDL, ensuring the original plan's logic is preserved even as the investigation evolves.
