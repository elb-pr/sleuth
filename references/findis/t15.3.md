Task 15.3, **Strategic and Tactical Assessments (NIM)**, produces law enforcement-oriented analytical products structured under the UK National Intelligence Model (NIM). These products translate the investigation's intelligence picture into either a long-horizon strategic assessment (informing force-level resource allocation and policy) or a current-period tactical assessment (driving immediate operational tasking decisions). The NIM framework is the mandatory standard for analytical product delivery within UK policing and allied law enforcement agencies.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. The NIM Product Hierarchy
The NIM defines five distinct analytical product types, each with a defined purpose, audience, and review cycle. The investigator must select the appropriate product type before drafting begins.
*   **Strategic Assessment:** A longer-horizon picture of the threat or problem landscape, typically covering a 6–12 month planning period. Used by force command to allocate resources, adjust priorities, and develop force strategy. Feeds the Control Strategy — the force's prioritised list of crime types and policing aims.
*   **Tactical Assessment:** A current-period (typically weekly or monthly) update on the operational threat picture. Used by tactical commanders and Tasking and Coordination Groups (TCGs) to drive immediate operational deployment. It identifies emerging threats, confirms ongoing patterns, and flags opportunities for enforcement action.
*   **Target Profile:** An intelligence picture focused on a specific individual or organised crime group identified as a priority for disruption or prosecution. This product type directly corresponds to the Subject and Corporate Profiles produced in Phase 3 (Task 8.1), contextualised within the NIM framework.
*   **Problem Profile:** An intelligence picture focused on a specific crime type, series, or problem area (e.g., a pattern of commercial burglaries in a defined area). Used to direct resources toward a defined problem and to identify the most effective intervention point.
*   **Subject Profile:** A detailed analytical profile of a specific individual — distinct from the Target Profile in that it may be used for protective (safeguarding) as well as enforcement purposes.

### 2. Strategic Assessment Structure
*   **Strategic Threat Picture:** A synthesis of current and emerging threats across the force area, with each threat assessed using ICD 203 probability language for likelihood of escalation or continuation.
*   **Pattern and Trend Analysis:** Long-run trends derived from the Chronological Matrix outputs (Phase 4, Task 9.4), identifying shifts in criminal methodology, geographic hotspot migration, and changes in network composition.
*   **Resource Implications:** An assessment of what the strategic threat picture demands in terms of investigative capacity, specialist capabilities, and inter-agency coordination. This section translates intelligence into resource planning language.
*   **Recommendations for the Control Strategy:** Specific recommendations on which crime types or intelligence requirements should be elevated or deprioritised within the force's strategic planning cycle.
*   **Source Base Assessment:** Consistent with Task 13.4, the strategic assessment includes a transparent statement of the intelligence source base underpinning it, including identified gaps that limit the assessment's scope or confidence.

### 3. Tactical Assessment Structure
*   **Current Threat Summary:** A concise summary of the most operationally significant intelligence from the current period — new subjects, emerging patterns, active operations. This is the tactical equivalent of the BLUF (Task 13.1).
*   **Emerging Threats and Opportunities:** Intelligence indicating new threats that require immediate response, and specific opportunities for enforcement action (e.g., a planned criminal event, a known subject in a fixed location, a time-sensitive vulnerability in a criminal network).
*   **Ongoing Operation Updates:** Status of active investigations and operations, with intelligence updates that may affect tactical decisions.
*   **Tasking Recommendations:** Specific, actionable recommendations for the TCG — surveillance taskings, arrest opportunities, disruption actions, inter-agency referrals. Each recommendation is accompanied by the supporting intelligence and an assessed likelihood of success.
*   **Outstanding Intelligence Requirements:** Active IRs from the ICP (Task 2.1) that remain unaddressed and should be prioritised in the current tactical cycle.

### 4. Integration with the Investigation's Analytical Products
NIM products are not produced independently from the investigation's analytical infrastructure — they are derived from it.
*   **Network Analysis Input:** The Network Architecture (Phase 4, Task 10.2) identifies Central Hubs and Bridge Nodes that inform Target Profile prioritisation and tactical disruption points.
*   **Chronological Matrix Input:** The temporal patterns identified in Phase 4 (Task 9.4) directly populate the pattern and trend sections of both strategic and tactical assessments.
*   **ACH Matrix Input:** The hypothesis testing in Phase 5 provides the evidential basis for the threat assessments in both product types. The most likely explanation from Task 12.3 becomes the threat assessment's principal judgment.

### 5. Quality Standards
*   **Tasking Linkage:** Every tactical recommendation must be traceable to a specific piece of intelligence. Unsourced tactical recommendations are not permitted.
*   **Review Cycle Compliance:** Under College of Policing Authorised Professional Practice, tactical assessments require review by an inspector or equivalent within defined timeframes. The review date and reviewing officer must be recorded in the case file.
*   **Separation from Operations:** NIM analytical products inform operational decisions — they do not make them. The language of the products must be framed as intelligence assessments and recommendations, not operational orders.
*   **No Covert Tactics Disclosure:** The same compartmentalisation principle that applies to legal summaries (Task 15.4) applies here — the source of intelligence derived from covert collection must not be disclosed in NIM products that may be shared beyond the immediate intelligence team.
