
Task 13.2, **Structural Separation of Facts, Assumptions, and Judgments**, enforces the objective architecture of the analytical briefing. The report must use distinct sections to prevent the reader from confusing verified intelligence with analytical interpretation — a conflation that can lead to misguided decisions.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Facts (Intelligence)
The Facts section presents verified data points drawn from the Entity Register, Evidence Register, and Source Register.
*   **Content:** Confirmed findings — entity identities, verified relationships, authenticated digital media, financial records, corporate filings, and other data graded 1 (Confirmed) or 2 (Probably True) on the Admiralty credibility axis.
*   **Source Citation:** Every fact carries an inline or footnoted reference to its Source Register entry and Admiralty grade. The reader can assess the evidential weight of each fact independently.
*   **No Interpretation:** The Facts section presents data without analytical commentary. "Entity A holds a directorship in Company B (Companies House filing, A1)" is a fact. "Entity A's directorship in Company B suggests they control the corporate network" is a judgment and belongs in the Judgments section.

### 2. Assumptions
The Assumptions section explicitly lists the suppositions used to bridge information gaps or frame the analytical argument.
*   **Content:** Every assumption identified during the ACH setup (Task 12.1) is listed with its rationale. For example: "This analysis assumes that the financial records obtained from Jurisdiction X are complete and have not been selectively disclosed."
*   **Impact Assessment:** Each assumption includes a brief statement of its impact on the conclusion. If the assumption is wrong, does the conclusion change? This links directly to the Sensitivity Analysis from Task 12.3.
*   **Visibility Requirement:** Assumptions must not be embedded within the narrative of the Facts or Judgments sections. They must be visually distinct — under their own header — so that the reader can evaluate how much of the conclusion rests on verified evidence versus unverified suppositions.

### 3. Judgments
The Judgments section presents the analytical conclusions derived from the synthesis of facts and assumptions.
*   **Content:** The analyst's interpretive conclusions — what the evidence means, what relationships it reveals, what patterns it suggests, and what the most likely explanation is. This section draws directly from the ACH conclusion (Task 12.3).
*   **ICD 203 Language:** All probabilistic claims within the Judgments section use standardised ICD 203 probability and confidence language. Non-standard hedging terms ("might happen," "good chance," "could be") are prohibited.
*   **Alternative Analysis:** The Judgments section must reference the competing hypotheses tested in Phase 5 and explain why they were rejected. This "Analysis of Alternatives" demonstrates that the conclusion was reached through structured reasoning, not confirmation bias.
*   **Gap Acknowledgement:** Judgments that are affected by unfilled intelligence gaps must be explicitly caveated. The reader must understand which conclusions are robustly supported and which are conditional on incomplete information.

### 4. The Separation Principle
The structural separation serves a defensive function — it makes the report auditable.
*   **Auditability:** A reviewer can independently verify each fact, challenge each assumption, and evaluate whether the judgments logically follow from the facts and assumptions presented. If the sections are blended, this audit becomes impossible.
*   **Legal Defensibility:** In disclosure or legal proceedings, the separation ensures that the prosecution or defence can distinguish between what was established through evidence and what was inferred by the analyst.
*   **Decision-Maker Protection:** By clearly labelling what is fact, what is assumed, and what is concluded, the report prevents the decision-maker from treating an analytical judgment as a confirmed fact — a conflation that can lead to costly or dangerous decisions.
