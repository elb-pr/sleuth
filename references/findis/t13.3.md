Task 13.3, **Applying Standardised Probability Language**, ensures that all probabilistic claims in the final briefing use the precise, calibrated vocabulary mandated by ODNI ICD 203. Vague or colloquial probability expressions ("might," "possibly," "there is a chance") are prohibited because they are interpreted inconsistently by different readers, undermining the reliability of the communication.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. The ICD 203 7-Tier Probability Scale
Every probabilistic claim in the report must be mapped to one of seven standardised tiers:
*   **Almost no chance / Remote:** 1–5%
*   **Very unlikely / Highly improbable:** 5–20%
*   **Unlikely / Improbable:** 20–45%
*   **Roughly even chance / Roughly even odds:** 45–55%
*   **Likely / Probable:** 55–80%
*   **Very likely / Highly probable:** 80–95%
*   **Almost certain / Nearly certain:** 95–99%

### 2. Usage Rules
*   **Tier Consistency:** The analyst must use terms from a single tier and must not mix terms from different tiers within the same sentence without a clarifying disclaimer. For example, "This is very likely, and almost certainly not a coincidence" mixes tiers and must be rephrased.
*   **Confidence Separation:** Confidence levels (High, Moderate, Low) and likelihood expressions must never appear in the same sentence. Confidence assesses the quality of the evidence; likelihood assesses the probability of the event. These are distinct dimensions that must be communicated separately to prevent reader confusion.
*   **No Non-Standard Terms:** Expressions such as "might happen," "good chance," "could be," "it is possible," "we believe," or "we think" are prohibited. Each of these terms is interpreted differently by different readers, defeating the purpose of standardised communication.

### 3. UK JDP 2-00 Alternative
In UK defence and intelligence contexts, the equivalent framework is documented in Joint Doctrine Publication 2-00 (JDP 2-00), which uses its own probability vocabulary:
*   Almost certain, Highly likely, Likely, Realistic possibility, Unlikely, Highly unlikely, Remote chance.
*   These terms map approximately (but not exactly) to ICD 203 tiers. The two frameworks must not be interchanged within the same report.

### 4. Application Throughout the Report
Standardised probability language is not confined to the BLUF — it must be applied consistently throughout the entire briefing.
*   **Judgments Section:** Every analytical conclusion carrying uncertainty is expressed using ICD 203 or JDP 2-00 terms.
*   **Source Summary Statement (Task 13.4):** Assessments of source base quality use ICD 203 confidence language.
*   **Findings Memo (Task 15.1):** Summary conclusions use the same standardised vocabulary.
*   **No Downward Translation:** Analysts must not "simplify" probability language for non-specialist audiences by reverting to colloquial terms. If a plain-language explanation is needed, the standardised term is stated first, followed by its percentage range in parentheses.

### 5. Quality Check
Before the report is finalised, a systematic sweep is conducted to verify compliance.
*   **Term Audit:** Every sentence in the report expressing probability or uncertainty is reviewed to confirm it uses an approved term from the appropriate scale.
*   **Confidence/Likelihood Conflation Check:** Every sentence is checked to ensure confidence and likelihood are not blended.
*   **Consistency Audit:** The probability expressions used in the BLUF are verified against the ACH inconsistency scores and Sensitivity Analysis to ensure they are mathematically justified.
