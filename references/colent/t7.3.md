Task 7.3, **Universal Metadata Tagging**, enforces the provenance and governance layer that makes the Entity Register legally defensible and analytically trustworthy. Every record in the register — whether created by deterministic matching, probabilistic resolution, or manual entry — must carry a standardised set of metadata fields that answer four questions: where did this data come from, when was it current, who processed it, and how confident are we in its accuracy?

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. Mandatory Metadata Fields
Four metadata categories are non-negotiable for every entity record and every relationship link in the register.

**Source Attribution:**
*   **Source Identifier:** The unique reference from the Source Register (Task 5.3) identifying exactly which source provided the data. Source attribution must be applied at the field level, not merely the record level — if a person's name came from Companies House and their address came from a LinkedIn profile, each field carries its own source reference.
*   **Source Admiralty Grade:** The alphanumeric grade (e.g., "B2", "F1") assigned during epistemic filtering (Task 3.1b), carried forward as metadata. This ensures that downstream analysts can instantly assess the evidential weight of any data point.
*   **Multi-Source Attribution:** When multiple sources contribute to the same field (e.g., three independent sources confirm a date of birth), all contributing source identifiers are recorded, along with the corroboration status.

**Temporal Metadata:**
*   **Date Observed/Collected:** The date on which the information was current or was collected. This is distinct from the date the record was created in the database. A company directorship filed in 2019 but discovered by the investigator in 2026 carries a `dateObserved` of 2019 and a `dateCollected` of 2026.
*   **Date Range (for Relationships):** Relationships may have start and end dates. A directorship held from 2015 to 2020 is recorded with both bounds. Open-ended relationships (current employment) carry a start date with no end date.
*   **Currency Flag:** Records older than a defined threshold (e.g., 12 months for address data, 24 months for employment data) are automatically flagged as potentially stale, triggering a re-verification requirement.

**Analyst Identity:**
*   **Create User:** The identity of the analyst who created the record. In automated extraction pipelines, this may be the system identifier plus the supervising analyst.
*   **Last Modified By:** The identity of the most recent modifier. Every modification is timestamped and attributed.
*   **Review History:** A log of every analyst who has reviewed, verified, or amended the record, creating an unbroken chain of analytical custody parallel to the physical chain of custody maintained in the Evidence Register.

**Confidence Scoring:**
*   **Entity Confidence:** The overall certainty that the record correctly identifies a specific real-world entity. High confidence is assigned to records with deterministic key matches (Task 6.3). Medium confidence applies to probabilistic matches confirmed by an analyst (Task 7.2). Low confidence applies to unresolved records with limited corroboration.
*   **Field-Level Confidence:** Where specific fields are less certain than others (e.g., a name is confirmed but a date of birth is inferred), confidence is scored at the field level as well as the record level.

### 2. Classification and Handling Controls
In investigations involving sensitive material, metadata must also govern who can access each record.
*   **Classification Level:** Records may carry sensitivity markings (e.g., Restricted, Confidential, Secret) that dictate access permissions within the investigation team.
*   **Handling Code (UK 5×5×5):** In UK policing contexts, the third dimension of the 5×5×5 system (Task 3.1b) specifies handling restrictions that are carried as record-level metadata in the Entity Register.
*   **Compartmentalisation:** Records derived from protected sources (e.g., covert human intelligence) may require compartmentalisation — visible only to authorised team members — with the restriction enforced at the metadata level.

### 3. Preventing Provenance Loss
The most critical failure mode in metadata management is the loss of source attribution during record merging.
*   **Merge Rule:** When two records are merged during entity resolution, the metadata from both original records must be preserved. Source attributions are combined, not overwritten. If Record A cites Source X and Record B cites Source Y, the merged record cites both.
*   **Field-Level Conflict Resolution:** If merged records contain conflicting values for the same field (e.g., different addresses), both values are retained with their respective source attributions and dates. The conflict is flagged for analyst review rather than silently resolved by overwriting one value with the other.
*   **Data Lineage:** The system must maintain a complete audit trail from any merged entity back to its original, pre-merge source records. This data lineage is the fundamental requirement for legal defensibility — if challenged, the investigator must demonstrate exactly how a composite entity profile was assembled from discrete sources.

### 4. Integration with Quality Checkpoints
The Phase 3 Quality and Integrity Checkpoints draw directly on metadata completeness.
*   **Source Audit:** Verify that no record has been "divorced" from its source attribution. Records with missing source fields are flagged as integrity failures.
*   **Stale Record Flagging:** Records whose `dateObserved` exceeds the currency threshold are surfaced for re-verification or marked as potentially outdated in any analytical output that references them.
*   **Duplicate Audit:** Metadata inconsistencies (e.g., two records with identical deterministic keys but different Create Users) may indicate unresolved duplicates that bypassed the resolution pipeline.
