Task 7.1, **Executing the Entity Resolution (ER) Pipeline**, is the methodological core of Phase 3. Entity resolution determines when fragmented, inconsistent, or differently formatted records refer to the same real-world entity. The theoretical foundation is the **Fellegi-Sunter framework** (1969), an established statistical model for record linkage that operates without requiring training data, relying instead on frequency distributions of agreed field values among matches and non-matches.

The detailed procedural breakdown of this task is as follows:

### 1. The Two Opposing Errors
Entity resolution is fundamentally a balancing act between two types of error, and the investigator must understand both before configuring the pipeline.
*   **False Positives (Over-Merging):** Erroneously conflating two distinct real-world entities into a single record. In investigative contexts, this can result in misattribution of criminal activity, wrongful accusation, or intelligence contamination. This is the more dangerous error.
*   **False Negatives (Under-Merging):** Failing to link fragmented records that refer to the same real-world entity. This means connections are missed and the investigative picture remains incomplete. This error is recoverable — missed links can be identified later through additional evidence or manual review.
*   **Calibration Principle:** In law enforcement and intelligence contexts, the pipeline is calibrated to minimise false positives at the expense of tolerating more false negatives. Precision is prioritised over recall.

### 2. The Fellegi-Sunter Framework
The Fellegi-Sunter model provides the mathematical basis for deciding whether two records should be linked, not linked, or flagged for manual review.
*   **Agreement Weights:** For each field compared between two records (e.g., name, date of birth, address), the model calculates a weight based on the probability that the field values would agree if the records were a true match versus the probability they would agree by random chance.
*   **Rarity-Based Weighting:** Agreement on rare attribute values carries greater weight than agreement on common ones. A match on an unusual surname like "Featherstonehaugh" is far more diagnostic than a match on "Smith." Similarly, agreement on a specific date of birth (e.g., 29 February 1984) is more significant than agreement on a common date.
*   **Composite Score:** The individual field weights are summed into a composite match score. Two thresholds are defined: a high threshold above which records are automatically linked (deterministic-equivalent confidence), and a low threshold below which records are declared non-matches. Records scoring between the two thresholds are routed to human review as "candidate matches."

### 3. The Resolution Pipeline Sequence
Entity resolution is not a single operation but a staged pipeline, with each stage progressively relaxing the matching criteria.
*   **Stage 1 — Deterministic Pass:** Exact-match on unique identifiers (already executed in Task 6.3). Records matched at this stage are committed with highest confidence.
*   **Stage 2 — Near-Deterministic Pass:** Matching on combinations of high-reliability fields (e.g., full name + date of birth + address). Minor variations (e.g., "St." vs "Street", middle initial present vs absent) are normalised before comparison.
*   **Stage 3 — Probabilistic Pass:** The Fellegi-Sunter model evaluates remaining unmatched records using weighted field comparisons. Fuzzy string matching algorithms (e.g., Jaro-Winkler distance, Levenshtein distance, Soundex/Metaphone for phonetic matching) are applied to handle spelling variations, transliteration differences, and data entry errors.
*   **Stage 4 — Human Review:** Candidate matches that fall between the automatic-link and non-match thresholds are presented to an analyst with a summary of agreeing and disagreeing fields, their individual weights, and the composite score.

### 4. Tooling
The pipeline can be implemented through a range of tools depending on scale and context.
*   **Enterprise Platforms:** Palantir Gotham, IBM i2 iBase, Quantexa, and DataWalk provide built-in entity resolution engines with configurable matching rules and visual review interfaces.
*   **Open-Source and Scripted:** **Splink** (UK Ministry of Justice, probabilistic record linkage at scale — millions of records on a laptop), **dedupe** (ML-based, active learning), **RapidFuzz** (C++ fuzzy matching), and **jellyfish** (Soundex, Metaphone, Jaro-Winkler for phonetic name matching) provide the building blocks for custom pipelines.
*   **Manual Resolution:** For small-scale investigations, resolution may be performed manually using spreadsheet comparison, OpenRefine clustering, or visual inspection — but the same logical principles (agreement weighting, threshold decisions, conflict logging) must be applied.

### 5. Configuration and Threshold Documentation
The specific thresholds and matching rules used must be documented as part of the investigation's methodology record.
*   **Threshold Justification:** The choice of automatic-link and non-match thresholds must be justified based on the investigation's tolerance for false positives versus false negatives. This documentation is essential for legal defensibility.
*   **Rule Transparency:** Every matching rule (which fields are compared, what normalisation is applied, what weights are assigned) must be recorded so that a third party can reproduce and audit the resolution decisions.
*   **Version Control:** If matching rules are revised during the investigation (e.g., because an initial configuration produced too many false positives), each version must be preserved with its associated rationale.
