Evidence & Material Register: Digital Chain of Custody Framework

1. Regulatory Framework & Standards Alignment

Aligning an evidence register with established legal and professional standards is a strategic mandate, ensuring that captured material remains admissible and defensible across legal, intelligence, and journalistic jurisdictions. By adhering to the UK College of Policing Authorised Professional Practice (APP) and the Berkeley Protocol on Digital Open Source Investigations, investigators establish a rigorous chain of custody that withstands the scrutiny of both adversarial legal proceedings and public accountability. This alignment transforms raw data into verified evidence by enforcing a synchronized methodology for capture, preservation, and evaluation.

Compliance Baseline: Investigative Standards

The following table maps the primary investigative standards found in global practice to their specific documentation and retention requirements:

Standard	Context	Core Documentation Requirements
APP / CPIA (UK)	Law Enforcement	Pursuit of all reasonable lines of enquiry; mandatory recording and retention of all material; maintenance of a disclosure schedule.
Berkeley Protocol	Human Rights / OSINT	Strategic documentation of the chain of custody, evidence archival, and adherence to principles of accuracy, transparency, and responsibility.
Admiralty System (NATO 6x6)	Intelligence / NATO	Alphanumeric grading of source reliability (A–F) and information credibility (1–6) to ensure standardized evaluation.

The "So What?" Analysis: Impact of Systemic Failure

Failing to maintain a structured evidence register introduces "Lost Evidence Chains," where digital material is collected without immediate archival, rendering it vulnerable to link rot or platform deletion. Without a synchronized register, the investigation risks "Source Conflation"—where multiple independent claims are merged into a single attribution, obscuring the primary origin of the material. In high-stakes environments, these failures act as active liabilities, potentially leading to the collapse of legal cases or catastrophic reputational damage due to an inability to prove the provenance and integrity of the findings.

This transition from high-level regulatory standards to operational reality is facilitated by the specific data architecture of the register.

2. Core Evidence Register Schema

A high-value material register requires a typed entity-and-link schema to prevent information silos during complex investigations. As an Investigative Systems Architect, I utilize a data model based on the POLE (Person, Object, Location, Event) framework. Crucially, we must distinguish between Semantic Types (categorical interpretations like "Person" or "Organization") and Entity Types (investigator-defined subcategories like "Suspect," "Witness," or "Subsidiary"). This distinction allows the database to interpret entities categorically while maintaining the specific investigative role assigned to each record.

Master Evidence Template: Minimum Required Fields

Every entry in the register must adhere to the following schema to meet legal admissibility requirements:

Field	Description	Requirement
Item Reference	Unique identifier (UID) for the specific item.	Mandatory
Description & Type	Detailed classification (Physical vs. Digital).	Mandatory
Source Attribution	Precise origin of the material (URL, Database, or Contact).	Mandatory
Handling Code	Access restriction per the 5x5x5 framework.	Mandatory
Capture Details	Date/Time (UTC) and identity of the collector.	Mandatory
Technical Identifiers	Cryptographic hash values (SHA-256).	Mandatory
Storage Location	File path or permanent Archive URL.	Mandatory
Admissibility Notes	Handling notes regarding legal or ethical parameters.	Recommended

Standard Optional Fields

Specific entity types require additional metadata to support deep-link analysis and entity resolution:

* Organizations: Registration number, country of incorporation, directors, beneficial owners, and parent/subsidiary relationships.
* Vehicles: Registration plate, VIN (Vehicle Identification Number), make/model, and sighting history.
* Images/Videos: Preservation of original EXIF metadata (GPS, camera serials) and frame-rate data.
* Web Materials: Full-page screenshots via tools like Hunchly to preserve the visual context of the URL at the time of capture.

Defining these fields ensures the structural integrity of the data, but the technical validity of the evidence relies on cryptographic verification.

3. Digital Integrity & Cryptographic Hashing Protocols

In modern digital forensics, technical verification is the anchor of the chain of custody. Cryptographic hashing is the non-negotiable proof that data has not been altered since the moment of acquisition. Beyond simple data capture, our methodology is guided by the JAPAN Principle: investigations must be Judicious (ethical), Analytical (structured), Passive (observation without interaction), Actionable (usable intelligence), and Notable (documented results).

Hash Verification Protocol

A robust protocol distinguishes between static and dynamic digital evidence to ensure archival integrity:

* Static Files: Documents and media must be hashed using the SHA-256 standard immediately upon acquisition.
* Web Content: To mitigate the risk of content deletion, an "archive-on-capture" policy is mandatory. All live URLs must be preserved using Hunchly or the Wayback Machine to create a permanent, timestamped record that exists independently of the live site.

Digital Quality Checklist for Investigators

* Normalize Timestamps to UTC: All capture times must be recorded in Coordinated Universal Time to ensure chronological synchronization across jurisdictions.
* Preserve EXIF Metadata: Original file metadata must be protected; tools that strip metadata during transfer must be avoided.
* Perform Periodic Hash Audits: Systematically re-verify stored files against their original hash values to detect data degradation or unauthorized access.

While technical integrity proves the data hasn't changed, the qualitative value of the material is determined by the Admiralty System.

4. Source Evaluation & Credibility Grading (The Admiralty System)

The NATO 6x6 (Admiralty System) is essential for accurate risk assessment. It demands the separation of Source Reliability from Information Credibility. This prevents the "Halo Effect," where an analyst assumes information is true simply because it comes from a reliable source, or ignores a valid lead because the source is untested.

The Source Grading Matrix

Source Reliability (A–F)

Code	Tier	Definition
A	Completely Reliable	No doubt about authenticity or competency; history of reliability.
B	Usually Reliable	Minor doubts; strong overall track record.
C	Fairly Reliable	Genuine doubt exists; some valid information in the past.
D	Not Usually Reliable	Significant doubt; occasional valid info.
E	Unreliable	History of providing invalid info; lacks trustworthiness.
F	Cannot Be Judged	No established track record (default for new sources).

Information Credibility (1–6)

Code	Tier	Definition
1	Confirmed	Independently corroborated; logical and consistent.
2	Probably True	Not confirmed; logically sound; consistent with other reporting.
3	Possibly True	Not confirmed; reasonably logical.
4	Doubtful	Possible but not logical; no comparative info.
5	Improbable	Illogical; actively contradicted by other reporting.
6	Cannot Be Judged	Insufficient basis for evaluation.

The "So What?" Analysis: Confidence Levels (ICD 203)

These grades dictate the Confidence Level of the final investigative findings as per ICD 203 standards. High confidence is assigned to findings supported by "A1" or "B1" material. Forensic Note: Per ICD 203, confidence levels (quality of evidence) and likelihood expressions (probability of an event) must never appear in the same sentence to avoid consumer confusion.

Once evaluated, evidence must be secured through strict access and handling protocols.

5. Handling Codes & Access Control

Handling restrictions are a strategic necessity to protect sensitive entities and maintain Operational Security (OPSEC). By classifying records, we ensure that the dissemination of material is proportionate to the risk of exposure.

The 5x5x5 Handling Framework

This framework defines how information is shared beyond the initial investigative team:

1. Code 1: Dissemination permitted within the organization and to external partners.
2. Code 2: Dissemination permitted within the organization and to specified partners.
3. Code 3: Dissemination permitted within the organization only.
4. Code 4: Dissemination restricted to specific named individuals or roles.
5. Code 5: Dissemination strictly prohibited without the express authority of the originator.

Analyst Identity Log

A comprehensive audit trail is required for every record. The Analyst Identity Log must record the identity of every individual who created, last modified, or accessed an evidence entry. This prevents "Source Conflation" by ensuring that if a discrepancy is found, the specific analyst and the source base they utilized can be isolated and reviewed.

Rigorous handling and logs lead to the final requirement: a continuous validation and audit schedule.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


6. Quality Assurance & Failure Mode Mitigation

Periodic audits are the final defense against investigative collapse. In high-stakes environments, "stale records" and "undocumented decisions" act as active liabilities. A systematic review process ensures that the register remains an accurate, living document.

Failure Mode & Mitigation Table

Potential Failure Point	Description	Mitigation Action
Duplicate Entities	Multiple records for the same real-world entity.	Entity Resolution Rule: Records sharing two or more independently verified identifiers (e.g., Email + Address) must be merged.
Lost Evidence Chains	Web content deleted before a permanent archive was made.	Mandatory "Archive-on-Capture" policy using Hunchly or the Wayback Machine.
Temporal Errors	Events recorded in incorrect timezones, corrupting the sequence.	Normalization of all timestamps to UTC; local conversions must be documented in notes.

Validation Schedule

The Lead Investigator must adhere to the following validation cycle:

1. Duplicate Audit: A systematic check for unresolved duplicates using the two-identifier resolution methodology.
2. Source Audit: Verifying that every record in the register has an attributed and graded source.
3. Currency Review: Flagging and removing "stale" or outdated information to ensure findings reflect current reality.

By implementing this framework, the investigative team ensures the delivery of an institutionally endorsed, defensible, and high-integrity investigative product.
