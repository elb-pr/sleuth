1. The Investigative Framework: Standardizing Entity Analysis

In the high-stakes environment of corporate due diligence and strategic intelligence, the transition from raw data to actionable intelligence is predicated on structural integrity. Standardized entity schemas serve as the architectural bedrock for forensic precision, ensuring data interoperability across disparate jurisdictions and toolsets. By adopting the POLE (Person, Object, Location, Event) model, investigators move beyond mere collection into the "So What?" layer—transforming fragmented findings into a structured intelligence database. This semantic standardization is critical for automated entity resolution, link analysis, and the mitigation of cognitive biases that arise from unstructured information.

1.1 The Core Entity Schema (POLE Framework)

The following schema defines the four primary entity semantics utilized in international investigative standards. This structure ensures that every record remains compatible with enterprise-grade analytical platforms (e.g., IBM i2 iBase) and open-source interoperability standards like schema.org.

Entity Semantic	Minimum Required Fields	Standard Optional Fields
Person	Full legal name, Date of Birth (DOB), Nationality, Gender.	Aliases, historical addresses, employment history, contact details, social media handles, physical description, ID documents.
Object	Organisation: Name, Registration Number, Country, Type. <br>Vehicle: Plate, Make, Model, VIN. <br>Account: Type, Identifier, Platform. <br>Document: Type, Title, Hash Value.	Organisation: Registered/trading addresses, Directors, UBO, Parent/Subsidiary links. <br>Vehicle: Owner(s), sighting history. <br>Account: Creation date, status. <br>Document: Source, Archive URL.
Location	Physical address or GPS coordinates, Type (Residential/Commercial/Other).	Grid references, country, historical names, geospatial polygons.
Event	Event Type, Date/Time (Normalized to UTC), Location Reference.	Participants, detailed description, associated POLE entities, outcome of activity.

1.2 Source Attribution and Metadata Standards

To maintain legal admissibility and prevent "Source Conflation"—where multiple reports of the same original data create a false sense of corroboration—field-level attribution is mandatory. Every record must carry the following universal metadata:

* Source Attribution: Explicit origin of the data (e.g., UK Companies House, specific leaked database).
* Confidence Level: Numerical rating (1–6) of the information’s credibility.
* Classification/Handling: Explicit instructions (e.g., TLP:RED, Private, Confidential) to prevent unauthorized disclosure.
* Date Observed/Collected: The timestamp of the information’s currency vs. its retrieval.
* Last Verified: The most recent confirmation of the data’s accuracy.
* Analyst Identity: Documentation of the investigator responsible for the record.

1.3 Methodological Note

Utilizing these standardized schemas allows for the seamless transition to the "Subject" and "Corporate" profiling modules. By anchoring investigations in a structured POLE framework, we ensure that personal associations and corporate control chains are analyzed through a consistent forensic lens.

2. Subject Profile Module: Identity, Employment, and Digital Footprint

A Subject Profile aims to achieve "Identity Resolution"—the definitive matching of a digital or documentary record to a real-world individual. This objective requires Reasonably Exhaustive Research, a standard mandating the exploration of all relevant records to minimize the risk of missing contradictory evidence. Adherence to the Berkeley Protocol is non-negotiable; investigators must ensure accuracy, preserve the subject's dignity, and maintain proportionality throughout the intelligence cycle.

2.1 Identity Verification and Professional Background

Identity resolution begins by anchoring the subject to unique identifiers to prevent "Identity Confusion" with similar names.

Category	Verified Credentials (Tiers 1–2)	Self-Published Claims (Tiers 3–6)	Admiralty Grade
Identity	Gov-issued ID, Tax IDs, Electoral Register.	Social media bios, personal websites.	[e.g., A1]
Employment	Statutory filings, Regulatory registers.	LinkedIn profiles, resumes, press releases.	[e.g., B2]
Licenses	Professional body databases (Law Society, GMC).	Industry affiliations, "Advisory" claims.	[e.g., A2]

2.2 Digital Footprint and SOCMINT Analysis (JAPAN Principle)

SOCMINT analysis must be conducted through the JAPAN Principle (Judicious, Analytical, Passive, Actionable, Notable). For the Investigative Architect, this involves building programmatic pipelines for username enumeration and metadata extraction.

1. Username Enumeration: Utilize CLI tools like Maigret (3,000+ sites) or Sherlock for recursive searches, prioritizing JSON/CSV output for integration into the master entity database.
2. Account Authentication: Use Holehe for email-to-account discovery via password-reset probing and GHunt for Google account footprinting.
3. Content Verification: Cross-reference media with known facts using reverse image search (Yandex/TinEye) and forensic metadata extraction (ExifTool).
4. Network Connection Evaluation: Analyze "friend" lists to identify real-world clusters. Use graph-based mapping to confirm identity via "Network Centrality" (identifying key bridges to the subject).

2.3 Legal and Regulatory History

Adversity is categorized by the Source Hierarchy (Tiers 1–8) to determine its weight in the final assessment.

Adversity Level	Source Type	Findings
Critical	Tier 1 (Official Gov. Records)	Criminal records, active sanctions, regulatory disqualifications.
High	Tier 2 (Regulated Disclosures)	Civil litigation (claimant/defendant), bankruptcy filings.
Moderate	Tier 4/7 (Secondary/Local)	Adverse media allegations, industry gossip, local news reports.

2.4 Analytical Transition

Personal history is rarely isolated. The Subject’s professional history and historical addresses serve as the primary gateway into the corporate entities they own, control, or represent.

3. Corporate Profile Module: Ownership, Control, and Financial Red Flags

Corporate investigations focus on identifying the Ultimate Beneficial Owner (UBO). Per FATF Recommendation 24, the UBO must always be a natural person who ultimately exercises control. Tracing this control requires piercing multiple layers of offshore vehicles and nominee arrangements designed to obscure the "So What?" layer of the investigation.

3.1 Corporate Identity and Structural Mapping

The core profile must include the Legal Name, Jurisdiction, and Registration Number. Investigators must map the hierarchy through Parent companies, Subsidiaries, and Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) to expose the full "Control Chain."

3.2 Beneficial Ownership and Control Tracing

Tracing continues until a natural person is identified. Control is defined by more than simple shareholding:

* Voting Rights: ≥25% voting power (standard threshold) or lower in high-risk scenarios.
* Veto Powers: Ability to block strategic decisions regardless of equity.
* Board Composition: Power to appoint/remove a majority of directors.
* Nominee Indicators: Identifying "Professional Nominees" through shared addresses or high-volume directorships (Tiers 3–5).

3.3 Financial Red Flag Registry

Based on the FATF standards and corporate forensics, the following indicators trigger enhanced due diligence:

* Circular Ownership: Detecting entities that ultimately own themselves through a loop (e.g., Co. A owns B, B owns C, C owns A).
* Shell Company Indicators: Minimal employees, "Virtual Office" addresses, and frequent changes in officers/address without operational justification.
* Unexplained Wealth: Inconsistency between stated revenue/success and the number of employees or industry presence.
* Jurisdictional Complexity: Use of high-secrecy offshore zones without a clear operational nexus.

3.4 Strategic Link

These structures are analyzed temporally to detect Anticipatory Restructuring—changes in ownership occurring just before legal or regulatory "Event" entities.

4. Advanced Analytical Synthesis: Timelines and Link Analysis

The value of an investigation is realized through chronological and relational synthesis. A "Raw Chronology" exposes the true sequence of events before a narrative is imposed, while link analysis reveals the hidden "Gatekeepers" of a network.

4.1 The Investigative Timeline and Gap Analysis

All events must be normalized to UTC, with the subject's local conversion documented to enable "Chronolocation" (e.g., verifying if a post matches local business hours).

Date/Time (UTC)	Local Time	Event Description	Source Ref	Temporal Certainty
YYYY-MM-DD	[Local Offset]	Factual account of event.	[Source ID]	Exact/Approx/Disputed

Gap Analysis Instruction: Identify periods with no evidential record. Assess if these are Evidential Absences (intentional "silence" often significant in fraud) or Investigative Gaps (missing data requiring further collection).

4.2 Link Analysis and Network Visualization

Link analysis should be interpreted through the following standardized Node Shapes and visual grammar:

* Visual Coding (Nodes):
  * Rectangle: Person.
  * Oval: Organisation.
  * Diamond: Event.
  * Triangle: Location.
  * Parallelogram: Vehicle/Object.
  * Hexagon: Account/Document.
* Visual Coding (Lines):
  * Solid Line: Confirmed relationship (e.g., statutory filing).
  * Dashed Line: Probable/suspected relationship (e.g., social media association).
* Centrality Analysis: Identify "Gatekeepers" (high betweenness centrality) and "Clusters" (coordinated sub-groups).

5. Quality Assurance and Source Evaluation Framework

To prevent Confidence Inflation, all reports must utilize the Admiralty System (NATO 6x6). This system ensures that source reliability (A–F) is never conflated with information credibility (1–6).

5.1 Source Reliability and Information Credibility (The 6x6 Matrix)

Rating	Source Reliability (A–F)	Information Credibility (1–6)
1 / A	Completely Reliable: History of total trustworthiness.	Confirmed: Corroborated by independent sources.
2 / B	Usually Reliable: Minor doubts; strong track record.	Probably True: Logically sound; consistent with others.
6 / F	Cannot be Judged: New/untested source.	Truth Cannot be Judged: No basis for evaluation.

5.2 Verification Protocols and Confidence Levels

Following the Three-Source Rule, critical findings must be corroborated by three independent sources before being labeled as "Confirmed."

The Source Hierarchy (Tiers 1–8):

* Tier 1: Official Gov. Records (Company/Land registries).
* Tier 2: Regulated Disclosures (Stock exchange filings).
* Tier 3: Verified Commercial Databases (Credit/News archives).
* Tier 4: Secondary Commentary (Established media/Trade press).
* Tier 5: Leaked Information/Data Breaches (Forensically verified).
* Tier 6: Social Media/Self-Published Content.
* Tier 7: Community/Local Media.
* Tier 8: General Gray Literature/Unverified Secondary Aggregators.

Confidence Levels:

* Confirmed (High): Verified by Tier 1 records or three independent sources.
* Probable (Moderate): Supported by reliable sources (Tiers 2–3) but lacks primary filings.
* Possible (Low): Single source or indirect evidence (Tiers 4–6).
* Unconfirmed: Reported but unverified.

5.3 Failure Mode Mitigation Checklist

Pitfall	Mitigation Strategy
Identity Confusion	Require ≥2 independent identifiers (e.g., DOB + Address) for a match.
Circular Reporting	Trace claims to the primary source to ensure "echoes" aren't counted as corroboration.
Context Stripping	Document the original date, location, and intent for every evidence item.
Confidence Inflation	Strictly separate Likelihood (Probability of event) from Confidence (Evidence quality).
