# Cultural Context & Ethnographic Background Assessment Template

1. Investigation Parameters & Epistemic Stance

In the execution of high-stakes socio-cultural investigations, the researcher is never a passive lens. Establishing a rigorous "Investigative Persona" and an explicit "Epistemic Stance" prior to data collection is a strategic necessity to ensure methodological alignment and legal defensibility. Failure to define these parameters invites inherent bias, where the investigator inadvertently projects their own cultural framework onto the target population, fundamentally compromising the reliability of the intelligence.

Investigative Persona Framework

The following roles define the operational stance and ethical priorities required for the assessment. Select the role that aligns with the mission objective:

* Academic Ethnographer
  * Stance: Long-term immersion and reflexive analysis. Prioritizes theoretical framing and the mapping of macro-social shifts.
  * Ethical Priority: Respectful relationships and the accessibility of findings to the host community.
* Humanitarian Needs Assessor
  * Stance: Participatory and community-based. Uses structured dialogue (disaggregated by gender/age) to identify protection risks and local capacities.
  * Ethical Priority: "Do No Harm" and the mobilization of local agency over mere data extraction.
* Cultural Intelligence Analyst
  * Stance: Operational decision-support. Focuses on understanding local norms and power structures to inform engagement strategies and predict mobilization.
  * Ethical Priority: Balancing obligations to the mission with the maintenance of operational security and subject safety.
* Ethnographic Journalist
  * Stance: Narrative immersion. Uses participant observation and life histories to elevate marginalized voices and challenge official narratives.
  * Ethical Priority: Openness, honesty, and the protection of source dignity under public interest justifications.

Generalization vs. Site-Specific Detail: The "So What?" Layer

The chosen role dictates the analytical resolution. An Intelligence Analyst may require actionable generalizations to support rapid field decisions, whereas a Humanitarian Assessor must insist on site-specific complexity to prevent the "instrumentalization" of culture. Without this distinction, the investigation risks "narrative bias," where the data is curated to support a pre-ordained conclusion rather than reflecting the ground reality.

Connective Tissue: These parameters form the foundational filter for the subsequent identity mapping, ensuring the data is processed through an appropriate ethical and operational lens.

2. Identity Structures & Social Stratification

Mapping identity is a dynamic process of negotiation, not a static classification of traits. Identities are constructed, contested, and shifted contextually. This section requires a multi-layered analysis of the community's social landscape.

Identity Dimensions & Mattering Maps

Map the following dimensions, utilizing "Mattering Maps" to identify how local identities connect to broader transnational networks:

* Ethnic Dimensions: Analyze internal diversity and shifting ethnic boundaries.
* Religious Dimensions: Distinguish between official doctrinal texts and local syncretic practices.
* Linguistic Dimensions: Use dialect mapping to infer historical settlement and current social affiliations.
* Class & Socio-Economic Status: Examine mobility and how education/occupation defines social clusters.
* Transnational/Diasporic: Map how digital platforms connect local actors to global grievances and financial flows.

3. Power Hierarchies & Institutional Governance

Investigators must distinguish between formal state power and informal community authority. Often, the visible government is secondary to informal networks that manage resources and resolve disputes.

Mapping Power Actors & Network Centrality

Categorize actors and apply Network Centrality Metrics to identify "Gatekeepers" (High Betweenness Centrality) and "Connectors" (High Closeness Centrality):

* Formal Leadership:
  * Government/Legal: Municipal authorities and police. Power is codified but often lacks local legitimacy.
  * Institutional: Schools and welfare agencies providing social control infrastructure.
* Informal Authority:
  * Patronage Networks: Individuals controlling resource distribution through personal loyalty.
  * Religious/Clan Leaders: Adjudicators of social norms; critical for gaining community entry.

Gatekeepers and Bridges: The "So What?" Layer

Identify "Bridges"—individuals like a Respected Matriarch who, despite lacking formal titles, possess high betweenness centrality. Identifying these hidden connectors is vital; they grant or deny Investigative Access. Bypassing them leads to "Gatekeeper Bias," where the investigator only sees the version of reality sanctioned by formal leaders.

Connective Tissue: The influence of these power actors is expressed through the enforcement of community norms.

4. Community Norms & Behavioral Expectations

Norms act as the "invisible infrastructure" of daily life. Understanding them is the difference between successful engagement and being shut out or endangered.

Normative Domains

* Gender Roles: Expectations for mobility and participation in public vs. private spheres.
* Hospitality/Negotiation: Rituals of rapport (e.g., tea-drinking) and "face-saving" communication styles.
* Grievance History: Past conflicts that color current interactions with "outsiders."

11-Tier Source Hierarchy for Normative Assessment

Rank evidence according to the following hierarchy derived from Source Context 2.2:

1. Peer-Reviewed Ethnographic Monographs: (Highest Reliability) Long-term, critically analyzed studies.
2. Institutional Ethnographic Archives: Oral histories and archived community studies.
3. Humanitarian/Community Profiling Assessments: Operationally focused (e.g., UNHCR profiles).
4. National/International Census/Demographic Data: Quantitative ethnic/linguistic backdrops.
5. Diaspora/Transnationalism Studies: Identity formation across digital/physical borders.
6. Cultural Institution/Heritage Publications: UNESCO-framed official understandings.
7. Community Media/Local Journalism: Internal narratives subject to local power dynamics.
8. Religious Doctrinal Texts: Normative teachings and authority structures.
9. Language/Dialect Mapping: Spatial data on settlement patterns.
10. Grey Literature/Practitioner Toolkits: NGO training materials; variable rigor.
11. Journalistic Ethnography: (Lowest Tier) Narrative reporting with news-value constraints.

Connective Tissue: Engaging with these norms requires adherence to rigorous "Do No Harm" protocols.

5. Role-Specific Ethical Constraints & "Do No Harm" Protocols

Methodology and ethics are inseparable. Unethical research introduces defensive bias and endangers both the subject and the investigator.

Berkeley Protocol Principles

All investigations must adhere to the six core principles of the Berkeley Protocol (Source Domain 1):

* Safety: Prioritize the physical and digital security of subjects and investigators.
* Accuracy: Distinguish established facts from inferences; document all decision-making.
* Dignity: Avoid intrusive data collection; respect subject human rights.
* Responsibility: Maintain an audit trail and be accountable for potential harms.
* Transparency: Methods must be documentable and reproducible.
* Proportionality: Information gathering must be limited to the mission objective.

Essentialization Audit

Prompt for User: "Identify and deconstruct three common stereotypes of this culture. You MUST cite a Tier 1 or Tier 2 source (from the hierarchy in Section 4) to provide evidence of the internal diversity or recent social change that contradicts these stereotypes."

Connective Tissue: Ethical rigor directly correlates to data reliability; safe sources are genuine sources.

6. Source Evaluation & Confidence Modeling

Distinguish between the probability of a cultural event and the quality of evidence supporting it.

The Admiralty System (NATO 6x6)

Letter	Source Reliability	Number	Information Credibility
A	Completely Reliable	1	Confirmed by other sources
B	Usually Reliable	2	Probably True
C	Fairly Reliable	3	Possibly True
D	Not Usually Reliable	4	Doubtful
E	Unreliable	5	Improbable
F	Reliability Cannot be Judged	6	Truth Cannot be Judged

ICD 203 Standards (Confidence & Probability)

Confidence Levels:

* High Confidence: Based on high-quality info (multiple Tier 1 sources) and sound reasoning.
* Moderate Confidence: Credible info but not fully corroborated; some gaps exist.
* Low Confidence: Significant gaps, questionable reliability, or fragile assumptions.

Probability Language Scale (Source Domain 3):

* Almost Certain: 95–99%
* Very Likely: 80–95%
* Likely: 55–80%
* Roughly Even Chance: 45–55%
* Unlikely: 20–45%
* Very Unlikely: 5–20%
* Remote: 1–5%

Crucial Rule: Do not mix probability terms (e.g., "Very Likely") with confidence levels (e.g., "High Confidence") in the same finding.

7. Synthesis & Investigative Implications

The final synthesis requires Triangulation—cross-referencing observations, interviews, and Tier 1 records to reach a defensible conclusion.

Strategic Summary

* Operational Opportunities: Identified "Bridges" or norms that facilitate mission goals.
* Cultural Red Lines (Risks): Taboos or grievances that trigger hostility (e.g., violating gendered private space).
* Identified Information Gaps: Explicitly list unknown variables (e.g., "Low confidence on youth sentiment in rural District X").

Final "So What?" Statement

This cultural map transforms raw data into a context-aware operation. By mapping the underlying layers of identity, power, and norms, the investigator can predict mobilization patterns, assess source credibility through the Admiralty system, and navigate complex human landscapes with ethical and operational precision. Cultural intelligence is the "interpretive layer" that ensures every other data point is actionable and defensible.
