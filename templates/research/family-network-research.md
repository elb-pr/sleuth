# Genealogical & Family Network Investigative Research Framework

1. Research Governance and GPS Foundational Strategy

Adopting a forensic mindset in genealogical research transitions the process from hobbyist lineage collection to a rigorous investigative discipline. The Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS) serves as the primary analytical infrastructure for resolving identity conflation and building a defensible family network. Forensic quality is achieved only when the investigator minimizes the probability of undiscovered contradictory evidence through reasonably exhaustive research. Every compiled tree and piece of family lore is treated as an unverified lead until re-derived from contemporary primary records, ensuring the final network is resistant to the "investigative failure modes" of presentism and anachronism.

1.3 GPS Compliance Checklist

The investigator shall certify compliance with the five elements of the GPS:

* [ ] Reasonably Exhaustive Research: Search of all relevant record types/jurisdictions to mitigate undiscovered contradictions.
* [ ] Complete and Accurate Citations: Every assertion followed by an Evidence Explained-style citation.
* [ ] Analysis and Correlation: Information graded for quality and compared across the entire evidence base.
* [ ] Conflict Resolution: All discrepancies (dates, parentage, locations) analyzed and explained.
* [ ] Written Conclusion: A proof argument demonstrating that the conclusion is reached through sound reasoning.

2. The Hierarchical Source and Evidence Matrix

Evidentiary integrity relies on a strict source hierarchy. Prioritizing original civil registrations over derivative, compiled genealogies is critical for determining the authoritative weight of a conclusion. Source grading dictates the threshold for entity resolution; a birth certificate filed near the time of the event carries significantly higher weight than an oral history recorded decades later.

2.1 Mandatory Source Hierarchy

Tier	Source Category	Authoritative Weight
1	Civil Registration (Birth, Marriage, Death)	High
2	Parish & Church Registers (Baptism, Burial)	High
3	Census & Population Registers	Medium/High
4	Probate & Wills (Administrations)	High
5	Land & Property Records (Deeds, Tax)	High
6	Immigration & Naturalization (Passenger Lists)	Medium
7	Military Service & Pension Records	Medium
8	Institutional Registers (Workhouse, Prison, Hospital)	Medium
9	Newspapers & Street Directories	Medium/Low
10	Compiled Genealogies/Online Trees	Low (Leads Only)
11	DNA Matches & Genetic Databases	High (Technical)
12	Oral History & Family Lore	Low (Hypothesis)

2.2 Evidence Classification Template

For each item of information, apply the following tri-part analysis:

* Source Type: Original (e.g., the actual register) vs. Derivative (e.g., a transcribed index).
* Information Type: Primary (first-hand knowledge) vs. Secondary (hearsay/compiled) vs. Undetermined.
* Evidence Type:
  * Direct: Explicitly answers the research question.
  * Indirect: Requires combination with other facts to infer kinship.
  * Negative: A documented absence where a record should exist (e.g., absence from a census to disprove residency).

2.3 NATO 6x6 Admiralty Grading Scale

All sources must be assigned an alphanumeric grade based on the NATO Intelligence Grading System (STANAG 2511).

Table I: Source Reliability | Code | Definition | Characteristics | | :--- | :--- | :--- | | A | Completely Reliable | No doubt about authenticity, trustworthiness, or competency. | | B | Usually Reliable | Minor doubts; strong track record of accuracy. | | C | Fairly Reliable | Genuine doubt; some valid information in the past. | | D | Not Usually Reliable | Significant doubt; occasional valid info only. | | E | Unreliable | History of invalid info; lacks trustworthiness/competency. | | F | Cannot Be Judged | No established track record (Default for new sources). |

Table II: Information Credibility | Code | Definition | Characteristics | | :--- | :--- | :--- | | 1 | Confirmed | Independently corroborated; consistent with other sources. | | 2 | Probably True | Not confirmed; logically sound; consistent with other reports. | | 3 | Possibly True | Not confirmed; reasonably logical; partially consistent. | | 4 | Doubtful | Possible but not logical; no other info to compare. | | 5 | Improbable | Illogical; actively contradicted by other reporting. | | 6 | Cannot Be Judged | Insufficient basis for evaluation. |

3. Forensic Entity Resolution and Kinship Hypothesis Testing

"Identity Confusion" is the primary failure mode in genealogical research. To resolve entities in regions with common patronymics, the investigator shall use the POLE (Person, Object, Location, Event) model to ensure Subject A is not conflated with a contemporary of the same name.

3.1 Entity Resolution Protocol

A "Confident Match" between two records requires a minimum of three independent identifiers to align:

1. Full Name & Known Aliases
2. Date of Birth (DOB) and Specific Locality
3. Occupation/Professional History
4. Spouse and Nuclear Family Members
5. Residential History (Address-level consistency)

5. Relational Link Analysis and Network Mapping

Family structures are defined by "Network Centrality." Identifying "Bridges" and "Gatekeepers" (e.g., witnesses who appear across multiple unrelated family records) often exposes hidden connectors between disparate lineages.

5.1 Typed Relationship Register

* FAMILY_OF: Direct biological or legal kinship.
* ASSOCIATED_WITH: Neighbors, business partners, boarders.
* OWNS: Legal claim to land or property.
* DIRECTOR_OF: Corporate or legal leadership roles.
* WITNESSED_BY: Presence at probate, marriage, or baptism.

5.2 Visual Grammar Instructions (i2 Standard)

When visualizing the network, the following node shapes and link weights must be utilized:

* Node Shapes:
  * Rectangles: Persons
  * Ovals: Organizations
  * Diamonds: Events (Marriages, Baptisms)
  * Triangles: Locations
  * Parallelograms: Objects (Wills, Deeds)
* Link Weights:
  * Solid Line: Confirmed relationship (Admiralty A1–B2).
  * Dashed Line: Probable/Unconfirmed (Admiralty C3–D4).
  * Dotted Line: Suspected/Low confidence (Admiralty E5–F6).

6. The Final Proof Argument and Conflict Resolution

A conclusion is only of forensic quality if it addresses and resolves contradictory evidence directly. Contradictions must be explained through source reliability, recency, and the informant's proximity to the event.

6.1 Conflict Resolution Protocol

* Discrepancy: [Describe the conflict, e.g., "1851 Census lists birth in York; 1861 lists birth in Leeds."]
* Reasoned Resolution: [State which source is preferred and why, e.g., "Informant for 1851 was the subject; 1861 was a head of household with secondary knowledge."]

6.2 Probabilistic Conclusion (ICD 203)

The investigator shall separate the likelihood of the event from the analytic confidence in the evidence base. These shall not appear in the same sentence.

* Likelihood Assessment (ICD 203 Scale): [Almost Certain (95–99%), Very Likely (80–95%), Likely (55–80%), Roughly Even Chance (45–55%), Unlikely (20–45%)]
* Analytic Confidence: [High, Moderate, Low]
* Example Proof Statement: "It is Almost Certain that Subject A is the biological father of Subject B [Source 1]. Analytic confidence in this assessment is High due to the corroboration of DNA triangulation and primary probate records."
