# Template Specification: 16-Section Cognitive Surrogate Profile

## Sections 1-10: Clinical and Personality Foundation

### Section 1: Personality Structure
**Framework:** Big Five / Five-Factor Model (Costa & McCrae, 1992)
Five dimensions, each continuous spectrum (low–high):
- **Openness:** Conventional/practical ↔ Curious/imaginative
- **Conscientiousness:** Flexible/spontaneous ↔ Disciplined/organised
- **Extraversion:** Reserved/solitary ↔ Sociable/assertive
- **Agreeableness:** Competitive/sceptical ↔ Cooperative/trusting
- **Neuroticism:** Emotionally stable ↔ Emotionally reactive

### Section 2: Attachment Architecture
**Framework:** Bartholomew & Horowitz (1991) Four-Category Model
Underlying dimensions (continuous):
- **Attachment Anxiety** (low → high): concern about rejection/abandonment
- **Attachment Avoidance** (low → high): discomfort with closeness/dependence

Prototypes: Secure (low/low), Preoccupied (high anxiety/low avoidance), Dismissing (low anxiety/high avoidance), Fearful (high/high)

### Section 3: Locus of Control
**Framework:** Levenson (1973) IPC Scale
Three independent dimensions (each low → high):
- **Internal (I):** Outcomes result from own actions
- **Powerful Others (P):** Outcomes controlled by other people
- **Chance (C):** Outcomes determined by fate/luck/randomness

### Section 4: Emotion Regulation
**Framework:** DERS (Gratz & Roemer, 2004)
Six facets of regulation difficulty (each low → high difficulty):
- **Awareness:** Difficulty attending to emotions
- **Clarity:** Difficulty understanding/differentiating emotions
- **Non-Acceptance:** Unwillingness to accept emotional responses
- **Goals:** Difficulty with goal-directed behaviour when distressed
- **Impulse:** Difficulty controlling behaviour when distressed
- **Strategies:** Limited access to effective regulation strategies

### Section 5: Defence Mechanisms
**Framework:** Vaillant (1977) Hierarchy
Four levels (least → most adaptive):
- **Level I — Pathological:** Psychotic denial, delusional projection, distortion
- **Level II — Immature:** Acting out, passive aggression, projection, fantasy, dissociation, splitting, idealisation, devaluation
- **Level III — Neurotic:** Intellectualisation, reaction formation, displacement, repression, undoing
- **Level IV — Mature:** Humour, sublimation, suppression, altruism, anticipation

Profile notation: Predominant level + most-used individual mechanisms.

### Section 6: Cognitive Distortions
**Framework:** Beck (1963; 1979); Burns (1980)
Ten distortions rated by frequency and domain (social/achievement):
All-or-Nothing Thinking, Catastrophising, Mental Filtering, Overgeneralisation, Personalisation, Emotional Reasoning, Should Statements, Labelling, Mind Reading, Discounting the Positive

### Section 7: Cognitive Triad
**Framework:** Beck (1967)
Three domains (negative → positive):
- **Self:** "I am worthless" ↔ "I am competent"
- **World:** "The world is hostile" ↔ "The world is manageable"
- **Future:** "Things are hopeless" ↔ "Things can improve"

### Section 8: Existential Orientation
**Framework:** Frankl (1946); Yalom (1980)
- **Meaning:** Nihilistic ↔ Local/constructed ↔ Universal/inherent
- **Agency:** Fatalistic ↔ Constrained ↔ Self-determined
- **Isolation:** Fundamentally alone ↔ Connected through shared experience
- **Mortality Salience:** Avoidant ↔ Aware ↔ Preoccupied

### Section 9: Contradiction Map
**Framework:** Kernberg (1984); Linehan (1993)
Template for coexisting opposing states. Standard axes: Connection↔Autonomy, Authenticity↔Adaptation, Trust↔Vigilance, Hope↔Cynicism, Self-worth↔Self-doubt, Vulnerability↔Control, Stability↔Stimulation

### Section 10: Predictive Risk Map
Trigger → Early Signal → Likely Response → Recovery Path. Populated empirically from observed patterns.

## Sections 11-16: Cross-Domain Synthesis Extensions

### Section 11: Cognitive Processing Architecture
**Framework:** Frederick (2005) CRT; Kahneman (2011) dual-process; Geirhos et al. (2020) shortcut learning

What existing sections miss: Section 6 captures what errors are made. This section captures how the processing architecture generates those errors.

**Dimensions:**
- **Reflective Override:** Accepts intuitive responses without checking ↔ Routinely detects and overrides intuitive errors
- **Processing Depth:** Surface (memorises features, matches patterns) ↔ Deep (extracts structure, tests generalisability)
- **Shortcut Reliance:** Relies on heuristics even when stakes are high ↔ Shifts to deliberative processing when stakes increase
- **Metacognitive Monitoring:** Low awareness of own processing mode ↔ High awareness; can report gut vs deliberation

**Cross-references:** Section 6 (distortions are outputs of processing architecture), Section 13 (low override predicts high illusory contingency), Section 15 (processing depth determines signal discrimination quality)

### Section 12: Behavioural Defaults Under Uncertainty
**Framework:** Timberlake & Lucas (1985) species-typical appetitive behaviour; Kahneman & Tversky (1979) prospect theory

What this captures: The psychological equivalent of Timberlake's species-typical appetitive behaviour. When contingencies are unclear, what does the person DO?

**Dimensions:**
- **Default Action Pattern:** Seeks information / seeks reassurance / withdraws / acts impulsively / freezes / talks it through / creates structure / reverts to routine
- **Uncertainty Tolerance:** How long they sustain goal-directed behaviour without clear feedback
- **Ambiguity Response:** Threatening (triggers anxiety) ↔ Stimulating (triggers curiosity)
- **Default Orientation:** Toward problem (approach) / away (avoidance) / toward others (help-seeking) / inward (self-monitoring)
- **Repertoire Breadth:** Narrow (always same default) ↔ Broad (matches default to context)

**Cross-references:** Section 1 (Openness predicts broader repertoire), Section 4 (DERS Goals facet predicts rapid abandonment under uncertainty), Section 2 (Preoccupied = help-seeking default; Dismissing = self-reliant default)

### Section 13: Contingency Sensitivity and Superstitious Cognition 🐦
**Framework:** Skinner (1948); Staddon & Simmelhag (1971); Timberlake & Lucas (1985); Justice & Looney (1990)

THE PIGEON SECTION. How readily the person forms and maintains spurious causal beliefs.

**Dimensions:**
- **Illusory Contingency Formation:** Rarely attributes causation without evidence ↔ Readily forms causal beliefs from temporal contiguity alone
- **Confirmation Bias Strength:** Actively seeks disconfirming evidence ↔ Selectively attends to confirming instances
- **Ritual Behaviour:** No ritualistic/superstitious habits ↔ Relies on rituals for perceived control
- **Extinction Resistance:** Rapidly stops behaviour when reinforcement ceases ↔ Continues long after reinforcement has ceased

**Signal detection framing:**
- **d' (sensitivity):** Can the person distinguish genuine causation from correlation in this domain?
- **β (criterion):** How much evidence do they require before accepting a causal claim?
- High d' + appropriate β = causal agent
- Low d' + low β = pigeon
- High d' + high β = overcautious

**Cross-references:** Section 3 (high Chance orientation increases superstitious cognition), Section 6 (personalisation and emotional reasoning are distortion-level expressions), Section 11 (low reflective override predicts high illusory contingency)

### Section 14: Interpersonal Strategy Profile
**Framework:** Balbuena, Kolla & Logan (2026); Axelrod & Hamilton (1981); Trivers (1971)

Not a fixed trait but a context-dependent policy characterised by parameters.

**Dimensions:**
- **Initial Stance:** Default cooperate or default defect
- **Responsiveness:** How quickly strategy shifts based on other's behaviour
- **Punishment Propensity:** How readily and how harshly defection is punished
- **Forgiveness Rate:** How quickly cooperation resumes after punishment
- **Strategic Flexibility:** Fixed strategy regardless of context ↔ Calibrated to each interaction

**Cross-references:** Section 2 (Attachment predicts initial stance), Section 5 (Immature defences predict harsh punishment), Section 13 (superstitious beliefs about what others "always" do)

### Section 15: Signal Discrimination and Epistemic Style
**Framework:** Geirhos et al. (2020); Nimpf et al. (2019)

How well the person distinguishes genuine signals from noise across domains.

**Dimensions:**
- **Source Discrimination:** Does not evaluate source credibility ↔ Automatically weights by reliability
- **First-Impression Anchoring:** Strong anchoring, slow to update ↔ Treats impressions as provisional
- **Anomaly Sensitivity:** Ignores inconsistencies ↔ Notices and investigates
- **Self-Narrative Replication:** Has not tested whether self-story holds under scrutiny ↔ Has actively examined and revised

**Cross-references:** Section 11 (reflective override is the mechanism; this section captures the epistemic policy), Section 7 (high anchoring + negative self-view = very difficult to shift), Section 13 (low anomaly sensitivity + high illusory contingency = robust superstition maintenance)

### Section 16: Approach-Avoidance Architecture
**Framework:** Fernandez (2020); Gray (1970) BIS/BAS; Elliot (2006)

Approach-avoidance is not a fixed trait but a modifiable gradient responding to both contingent and noncontingent environmental features.

**Dimensions:**
- **Approach Targets:** What the person moves toward (novelty, intimacy, achievement, stimulation, control, understanding, comfort)
- **Avoidance Targets:** What the person moves away from (rejection, failure, uncertainty, conflict, vulnerability, boredom, loss of control)
- **Gradient Rigidity:** Fixed regardless of context ↔ Context-dependent
- **Elicited vs Deliberate:** Primarily automatic ↔ Primarily conscious choice
- **Approach Threshold:** How much environmental support needed before approach emerges
- **Avoidance Generalisation:** Specific (this person, this context) ↔ Generalised (all contexts of this type)

**Cross-references:** Section 2 (attachment avoidance is a specific case), Section 12 (default orientation is approach-avoidance under uncertainty), Section 10 (risk map triggers are events that shift the gradient toward avoidance)

## How Each Section Informs Interaction

| Section | Use in Conversation |
|---------|-------------------|
| S1 Big Five | Match communication style: high Openness gets abstract framing, high Conscientiousness gets structured options, high Neuroticism gets gentler challenge |
| S2 Attachment | Anticipate relational reactions: preoccupied users need reassurance before challenge, dismissing users respond to respect for autonomy, fearful users need safety before depth |
| S3 Locus of Control | Frame agency: high Internal users respond to "you can change this", high Powerful Others respond to "who could help", high Chance users need evidence that outcomes are influenceable |
| S4 DERS | Calibrate emotional load: high Goals difficulty means pausing complex tasks when distress is present, high Impulse means slowing decision pace, low Strategies means offering concrete regulation tools |
| S5 Defence Mechanisms | Read the room: intellectualisation signals emotional avoidance, projection signals perceived threat, humour signals either processing or deflection — match response to which |
| S6 Cognitive Distortions | Gently counter in real-time: when catastrophising appears, ground in base rates; when all-or-nothing appears, introduce middle positions; when personalisation appears, broaden attribution |
| S7 Cognitive Triad | Monitor wellbeing trajectory: negative self-view + negative future = high risk for hopelessness; positive self + negative world = frustration pattern; track shifts across sessions |
| S8 Existential Orientation | Match depth: nihilistic users respond to local meaning-making, not grand purpose; high agency users respond to action framing, not passive reflection |
| S9 Contradiction Map | Hold both poles: when the user is oscillating, reflect both sides rather than collapsing to one; name the axis if they seem stuck |
| S10 Predictive Risk Map | Early warning: if conversation topic approaches a known trigger, prepare for the predicted response pattern and have the recovery path ready |
| S11 Cognitive Processing | Match challenge type: high reflective override gets Socratic questioning, low override gets concrete examples and explicit reasoning scaffolds |
| S12 Behavioural Defaults | Predict stuck patterns: if the user's default under uncertainty is freeze, offer micro-steps; if it's impulsive action, slow the pace; if it's help-seeking, provide options rather than answers |
| S13 Contingency Sensitivity | Pigeon detection: see references/pigeon-mechanics.md |
| S14 Interpersonal Strategy | Anticipate conflict patterns: high punishment propensity means carefully framing disagreement; low forgiveness means acknowledging ruptures explicitly |
| S15 Signal Discrimination | Adjust evidence presentation: high source discrimination gets cited claims, low gets fewer options presented more clearly; high anchoring gets reframing before new information |
| S16 Approach-Avoidance | Frame recommendations: high approach threshold means breaking big moves into small testable steps; high avoidance generalisation means distinguishing this situation from the pattern |
