This governance framework facilitates the "Normalization" of disparate datasets, allowing for the subsequent identification of long-term economic path dependencies and regional growth drivers.

2. Macro-Economic Trajectory & Regional Resilience

Situating a specific site within its broader Functional Economic Area (FEA) is critical for identifying whether evolution was driven by "Localization Economies" (industry-specific agglomeration) or "Urbanization Economies" (diversified city-scale advantages). By applying theories of Agglomeration and Growth Poles, we evaluate the site's role as a dynamic center. This perspective determines if the site generated "spread effects" that catalyzed regional growth or "polarization" that drained resources from the periphery, creating a trajectory of path dependency.

Industrial Structure Evolution The site’s trajectory illustrates a shift from an industrial growth pole—dependent on manufacturing productivity—to a fragmented service base. Using a path-dependency framework, we analyze how the site became "locked-in" to declining industrial structures. The failure of the 1985 policy shift to fully regenerate the area suggests that historical specialization created a structural "friction" that the service economy has struggled to overcome.

5. Social Dynamics & Institutional Context

"History from Below" provides the human context for abstract spatial econometric data, analyzing how social stratification and institutional encounters influenced the site's adaptive capacity.

Social Stratification & Mobility

* Occupational Structure: Historic dominance of skilled manual labor created a rigid social structure that limited mobility once the industrial growth pole collapsed.
* Housing Policy: The shift from employer-tied housing to "Right to Buy" fragmented the historical community fabric, reducing the bonding capital necessary for collective resilience.
* Migration and Resilience: Post-war labor migration initially provided a "Spread Effect" of cultural capital, though subsequent economic decline led to out-migration of the highly skilled.

Institutional Footprint

* Parishes and Schools: Served as the primary mechanisms of Social Capital, maintaining community cohesion during deindustrialization.
* Welfare and Social Control: The 1960s expansion of state health and welfare institutions functioned as Social Control, managing the consequences of the declining industrial base.

Community Perspective Synthesis Oral histories challenge the "success" narratives found in official 1980s regeneration reports. While the "Regenerated Buffer" looks successful in planning documents, community media highlights "displacement," suggesting that the economic "Urbanization Economies" did not benefit the original "Artisan Fringe" population.
